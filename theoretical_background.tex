\section{The general theory of relativity}\label{theory_sec:GR}
The general theory of relativity introduces a geometric interpretation of gravity, resting on a set of geometric assumptions and principles evolved from the older classical Newtonian theory of gravity and relativity. The first of these is the \textbf{weak equivalence principle} which states that \textbf{' the inertial mass and gravitational mass of any object are equal'}. Einstein reformulated this statement to fit the language of the special theory of relativity by stating that \textbf{'In small enough regions of spacetime, the laws of physics reduce to those of special relativity; it is impossible to detect the existence of a gravitational field by means of local experiments.'} The consequences of this principle are that freely falling frame of reference cannot recognize that freely falling test particles in the vicinity of the frame are being accelerated (or under the influence of gravity). The consequences of this principle invites the formulation of a geometric theory of gravity where gravity manifests itself as curvature. 

To have a mathematical theory where observation could be interpreted, Einstein used differentiable manifolds to describe the four dimensional spacetime. Differentiable manifolds allow a definition of physical quantities such as position and momentum along with a continuous derivative of these quantities on the manifolds. Having a differentiable quantity allows also to introduce conservation laws which are a direct results of symmetries in a particular problem \citep{Noether_1983}. Therefore, to describe the motion of particles in a curved spacetime, the metric tensor is defined on a non-Riemannian manifold. The metric tensor is a solution for the Einstein field equations which are second order differential equations on $\mathbb{R}^{4\times4}$. 

The metric tensor $g_{\nu \mu}$ (in Einstein index notation) is a $4 \times 4$ symmetric tensor that describes the physical length of an infinitesimal line given a particular system of coordinates such that 

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{d}s^2 = g_{\nu \mu} \mathrm{d}x^{\nu} \mathrm{d}x^{\mu},
\end{equation}
where $\{\mathrm{d}x^\mu\}_{0, 1, 2, 3}$ is a basis for the contangent bundle of the manifold. In classical terms, $\{\mathrm{d}x^\mu\}_{0, 1, 2, 3}$ is the differential along a coordinate axis $\{x^\mu\}_{0, 1, 2, 3}$ For a generalized space time, the path of a free particle is described by the geodesic equation. If the coordinates of a particle in spacetime $x^\mu$ is parameterized with an affine parameter $\lambda$, then the coordinates $x^{\mu} (\lambda)$ is constrained by an ordinary differential equation, namely the geodesic equation given as:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:geodesic}
    \frac{\mathrm{d}^2x^\mu}{\mathrm{d}\lambda^2} + \Gamma^\mu_{\rho\sigma} \frac{\mathrm{d}x^\rho}{\mathrm{d}\lambda} \frac{\mathrm{d}x^\sigma}{\mathrm{d}\lambda} = 0,
\end{equation}
where $\Gamma^\mu_{\rho\sigma}$ are the Christoffel symbols given in terms the metric tensor such that

\begin{equation}
    \Gamma^\sigma_{\mu\nu} = \frac{1}{2} g^{\sigma\rho} \left( \partial_\mu g_{\nu\rho} + \partial_\nu g_{\rho\mu} - \partial_\rho g_{\mu\nu} \right)  .  
\end{equation}

In the Newtonian limit, the gravitational potential $\Phi$ is related to the mass density $\rho$ in  the following equation
\begin{equation} \label{eq:potential}
    \nabla^2 \Phi = 4\pi G \rho,
\end{equation}
with $G$ being the gravitaional constant. To formulate a theory which reduces to Eq. \ref{eq:potential}, we define the energy-momentum tensor $T_{\mu \nu}$ which is a a $4 \times 4$ symmetric tensor that describes the energy density and shear in the four-dimensional spacetime. Since the gravitational field manifest itself as curvature, the analogy to Eq. \ref{eq:potential} motivate a proportionality relationship between the second derivative of the metric tensor $g_{\mu \nu}$ and the stress-energy tensor $T_{\mu \nu}$ 
\begin{equation}
    \left[ \nabla^2 g \right]_{\mu\nu} \propto T_{\mu\nu}.
\end{equation}
A geometric quantity that is composed of the second derivatives of the metric tensor is the Ricci tensor $R_{\mu \nu}$ expressed in terms of the Christoffel symbols
\begin{equation} \label{eq:Ricci}
    R_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\lambda \Gamma^\lambda_{\nu\mu} 
           - \partial_\nu \Gamma^\lambda_{\lambda\mu} 
           + \Gamma^\lambda_{\lambda\rho}\,\Gamma^\rho_{\nu\mu} 
           - \Gamma^\lambda_{\nu\rho}\,\Gamma^\rho_{\lambda\mu}.
\end{equation}
Meanwhile, the Ricci scale is defined as the contraction of the Ricci tensor
\begin{equation} \label{eq:Ricci_scalar}
    R = R^\mu_{\ \mu} = g^{\mu\nu} R_{\mu\nu}.
\end{equation}

Therefore, a possible relationship between the stress-energy tensor and the Ricci tensor is
\begin{equation} \label{eq:EFE_guess}
    R_{\mu\nu} = \kappa T_{\mu\nu}.
\end{equation}
To find $\kappa$, the conservation of energy is invoked as  
\begin{equation}
    \nabla^\mu T_{\mu\nu} = 0,
\end{equation}
where $\nabla^\mu$ is the covariant derivative. Following Eq. \ref{eq:EFE_guess} and using Eq. \ref{eq:Ricci} and Eq. \ref{eq:Ricci_scalar}, the covariant derivative of the Ricci tensor is
\begin{equation}
    \nabla^\mu R_{\mu\nu} = \frac{1}{2} \nabla^\mu R g_{\mu\nu}.
\end{equation}    
Therefore, we can define another tensor $G_{\mu \nu}$ with a vanishing covariant derivative
\begin{equation}
    G_{\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2} R g_{\mu\nu}.
\end{equation}
$G_{\mu\nu}$ is called the Einstein tensor, and the Einstein's field equation regardless
\begin{equation} \label{eq:EFE}
    G_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu},
\end{equation}
where I used $\kappa = 8\pi G/c^4$ which can be obtained by reducing Eq. \ref{eq:EFE} to the Newtonian limit in Eq. \ref{eq:potential}. Einstein modified Eq. \ref{eq:EFE} to account for a vacuum solution by adding a term to represent the vacuum energy $\Lambda g_{\mu \nu}$ where $\Lambda$ is called the cosmological constant, and the final equation becomes
\begin{equation} \label{eq:EFE_full}
    G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu \nu} = 8\pi G T_{\mu\nu}.
\end{equation}



Finally, the 4-velocity of a particle in general relativity is defined as 
\begin{equation} \label{eq:4-velocity}
    U^{\mu} = \frac{\mathrm{d}x^{\mu}}{\mathrm{d}\lambda}.
\end{equation}
Therefore, the 4-velocity would follow the normalization
\begin{equation}
    g_{\mu \nu}U^{\mu}U^{\nu} = -1,
\end{equation}

and the 4-momentum for a particle of mass $m$ is defined as
\begin{equation}
    P^{\mu} = m U^{\mu},
\end{equation}
and the normalization of momentum is 
\begin{equation} \label{eq:momentum_noermal}
    g_{\mu \nu}P^{\mu}P^{\nu} = -m^2.
\end{equation}
Notice that either of the previous normalization is invariant under coordinate transformation. We define the observed energy of a particle of 4-momentum $P^{\mu}$ with respect to an observer with 4-velocity $U^{\mu}$ such that it reduces to the special relativistic limit if the particle has a vanishing spatial velocity and the observer 
\begin{equation} \label{eq:energy}
    E_{obs} = -g_{\mu \nu} U^{\mu} P^{\nu}.
\end{equation}




The Einstein theory of general relativity can explain cosmological observations such as cosmological redshift and the expansion of the universe where the expansion is driven by the energy content in the universe. Though the FLRW metric (explained in \ref{theory_sec:FLRW}) is phenomenologically motivated, the variation of the scale factor with conformal time $a$ is described by the Einstein's field equation. 




\section{The Friedmann-Robertson-Lemaître-Walker metric} \label{theory_sec:FLRW}
To describe the entire universe using one metric tensor requires educated simplifications and assumptions. The first guiding principle is the Copernican principle \citep{bondi2010cosmology} which states that \textbf{'we do not occupy a special place in space'}. The Copernican principle motivates the cosmological principle which states that 'the universe is homogeneous and isotropic'. Early galaxy surveys have shown that the universe is only homogeneous on large scales, therefore, the full cosmological principle is stated as \textbf{'the universe is homogeneous and symmetric on large scales'}. An additional assumption that simplifies that narrows down the possible formula for a metric is Weyl's postulate which states that \textbf{'the world lines of the galaxies form a 3-sheaf of non-intersecting geodesics orthogonal to layers of spacelike hypersurfaces'}. Weyl's postulate, coupled with the cosmological principle, allows a better definition of where homogeneity and isotropy apply, namely that the universe is 'spatially homogeneous and isotropic'. Therefore, a maximally symmetric metric that follows both principles takes the form
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{d}s^2 = -c^2 \mathrm{d}t^2 + g_{ij} \mathrm{d}x^i \mathrm{d}x^j,
\end{equation}
where $t$ is cosmic time. A two-dimensional schematic diagram of this metric is shown in \ref{theory_fig:world_lines}. 
\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{figs/world_lines.png}
    \caption{A schematic representation of spacetime that follows Weyl's postulate. The green lines labelled with letters are world lines, the path of freely falling objects in the spacetime (or geodesics). The redlines are spacelike hypersurfaces labelled with the same cosmic time $t_i$. These hypersuraces are homogeneous and isotropic.}
    \label{theory_fig:world_lines}
\end{figure}


Using the tools of differential geometry, a maximally symmetric metric that also accounts for a homogeneous expansion of the universe is 
\begin{equation} \label{eq:flrw}
    \mathrm{d}s^2 = -c^2\mathrm{d}t^2 + a^2(t) \mathrm{d}s_3^2,
\end{equation}

where $a(t)$ is the scale factor that couples the spatial part with time and the spatial part, $c$ is the speed of light, $\mathrm{d}s_3^2$ represent a volume element in a maximally symmetric space that takes the following form in hyperspherical coordinates
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sphere3}
    \mathrm{d}s_3^2 = \mathrm{d}\chi^2 + S_k(\chi)^2 \left( \mathrm{d}\theta^2 + \sin^2 \theta \, \mathrm{d}\phi^2 \right),
\end{equation}
the function $S_k(\chi)$ takes one of three forms by virtue of the number $k$
\begin{equation}
S_k(\chi) = 
\begin{cases} 
    \frac{1}{\sqrt{k}} \sin(\sqrt{k} \chi) & \text{if } k > 0, \text{for a closed universe}\\
    \chi & \text{if } k = 0, \text{for a flat universe}\\
    \frac{1}{\sqrt{|k|}} \sinh(\sqrt{|k|} \chi) & \text{if } k < 0, \text{for an open universe}
\end{cases}.
\end{equation}
The case when $k = 0$ represents a flat universe, where the spatial part reduces to the usual spherical coordinates. The flat FLRW metric reads
\begin{equation} \label{eq:FLRW}
    \mathrm{d}s^2 = -c^2\mathrm{d}t^2 + a^2(t) \left[ \mathrm{d}\chi^2 + \chi^2 \left( \mathrm{d}\theta^2 + \sin^2 \theta \, \mathrm{d}\phi^2 \right)\right].
\end{equation}

The coordinate $\chi$ could be understood by considering two space like points in spacetime, one with $\chi = 0$ and another with $\chi = \chi_c$ leading To

\begin{equation}\label{eq:comoving}
    s = a(t) \chi_c, 
\end{equation}
which means that the physical distance changes with time for a given $\chi_c$. We call $\chi_c$ the comoving distance. Taking the derivative with respect to cosmic time in Eq. \ref{eq:comoving}, we can retrieve the empirical law that \citet{Hubble_1929} discovered by recording the relationship between the distance to galaxy and the speed of the galaxy
\begin{equation}
   \frac{\mathrm{d}s}{\mathrm{d}t} = V = \frac{\dot{a}}{a} s,
\end{equation}
with $\dot{a} = \mathrm{d}a/\mathrm{d}t$. We define the Hubble parameter at a cosmological time $t$ such that
\begin{equation} \label{eq:hubble}
    H(t) = \frac{\dot{a}}{a}.
\end{equation}

Another distance indicator is gravitational redshift, similar to doppler redshift, gravitational redshift is the modulation of light frequency as light travels across the universe. If a fundamental observer at cosmic time $t_{0}$ emits a burst of light, then the energy of this light particle is given by  Eq. \ref{eq:energy}. If another fundamental observer observes this light at a later time $t$, the ratio between the two energies is defined as
\begin{equation}
    1 + z = \frac{E_e}{E_o},
\end{equation}
where $E_e$ and $E_o$ are the energy of light measured by the emitter and the observer respectively, and \textbf{$z$ is called the cosmological redshift}. $z$ is related to the wavelength of light in the limit where each of the emitter and the observer are fundamental observers, meaning that their 4-velocity is $U^{\mu} = (1, 0, 0, 0)$. Therefore, the light energy could be expressed using Planck's law \citep{Planck1900b},  $E = h/\lambda$, with $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{J·s}$ is Planck's constant, and $\lambda$ is the wavelength of light. The final relationship describing the modulation in the observed wavelength of light is 
\begin{equation} \label{eq:redshift_freq}
    1 + z = \frac{a(t_o)}{a(t_e)} = \frac{\lambda_o}{\lambda_e},
\end{equation}
with $\lambda_e$ and $\lambda_o$ are the light wavelength measured by the emitter and the observer respectively. This relationship could be exploited by quantifying the modulation of the wavelength and its relationship to the scale factor. In a nutshell, a light emitted at a scale factor $a(t_e)$ travels through an expanding universe until measured by another observer at time $t_o > t_e$ corresponding to $a(t_o) > a(t_e)$, therefore $\lambda_o > \lambda_e$, which explains why distant sources are redshifted. In practice, by measuring the spectrum of light of a distant galaxy and convolving it with a standard spectrum (the expected spectrum of galaxy in the nearby universe), it is possible to obtain the redshift of the distant galaxy. If we choose $a = 1$ in the current epoch, the relationship between redshift and the scale factor reduces to
\begin{equation} \label{eq:redshift}
    a = \frac{1}{1 + z},
\end{equation}
where $z = 0$ refers to the current epoch. Since light moves along a null geodesic, it is possible to obtain the comoving distance to an emitter given the redshift of the emitter. Starting from Eq. \ref{eq:FLRW}, and working in a coordinate system with the light travelling along $\theta = 0 $ and $\phi = 0$, the null geodesic dictates that 
\begin{equation}
    c\mathrm{d}t = a(t)\mathrm{d}\chi,
\end{equation}
which could be further arranged using Eq. \ref{eq:hubble} and Eq. \ref{eq:redshift} into
\begin{equation} \label{eq:comoving_distance}
    \chi = \int_{1}^{z_e} \frac{c}{H(z)} \mathrm{d}z.
\end{equation}
From the perspective of Eq. \ref{eq:redshift}, the cosmological redshift can be used as proxy of time where larger value of redshfit conrreponds to smaller value of the scale factor $a$. Meanwhile from the perspective of Eq. \ref{eq:comoving_distance}, the cosmolgoical redshift is proxy for distance where sources of larger redshift correspond to larger distance. In a cosmological galaxy survey, measuring distances from the Earth towards galaxies is not a trivial task, however, the cosmological redshift can be measured by measuring the deviation the electromagnetic spectrum of a galaxy from the expected spectrum of a galaxy at redshift $z = 0$ \footnote{Of course, the process of measuring redshifts is cumbersome in practice, and this statemetn is meant to explain the basic principle behind measring the cosmological redshift.}. 

Another distnace scale of interest is the Hubble scale. The Hubble scale is the distance at which the recession velocity of a galaxy equals the speed of light. Using Hubble's law, the Hubble scale is given by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hubble_scale}
    s_H = \frac{c}{H}.
\end{equation}
The Hubble scale is a distance scale that is used to define the size of the observable universe. It has a significant consequences on the growth of perturbations in the universe, as will be discussed in \ref{theory_sec:BF}.


Throughout this chapter, a question is lurking behind all the kinematics of the universe that the FLRW metric proposes: how does the scale factor $a$ change with cosmic time? In principle, this requires using the Einstein's field equation Eq. \ref{eq:EFE_full} along with assumptions about the form of the stress energy tensor $T^{\mu \nu}$. Assuming that the energy density in the universe is that of a perfect fluid, solving the Einstein's field equation for $a$ yields 
\begin{subequations}
\label{eq:Friedmann}
\begin{align} 
    \frac{\ddot{a}}{a} &= -\frac{4\pi G}{3} \left( \rho + \frac{3p}{c^2} \right) + \frac{\Lambda c^2}{3} \label{eq:Friedmann1} \\
    \frac{\dot{a}^2}{a^2} &= \frac{8\pi G}{3} \rho + \frac{\Lambda c^2}{3} - \frac{kc^2}{a^2}, \label{eq:Friedmann2}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
with $p$ being the isotropic pressure of the cosmic fluid and $\rho$ being the density \footnote{Since energy and mass are equivalent up to the squared of the speed of light, we may refer to the different constituents of the universe as 'contributing to the energy budget of the universe'.}. These equations are known as the Friedmann equations, describing the evolution of the scale factor $a$ with respect to cosmic time $t$. To solve these equations, a relationship between the pressure p and the energy density is needed, known as the equation of state. The possible components that contribute to the energy density $\rho$ have been a process of trial and error starting de Sitter universe \citep{deSitter_1917} which is a universe with no matter up to the recently speculated model with dynamical dark energy \citep{adame2025desi}. Eqs. \ref{eq:Friedmann} were derived with the assumption of a homogeneous and isotropic universe on all scales; this is not the case when studying the inhomogeneous universe with large-scale structure. Therefore, the physical quantities in Eqs. \ref{eq:Friedmann} are only a functions of time (the total density $\rho(t)$ and the isotropic pressure $p(t)$).In \ref{theory_sec:cosmo_model}, I briefly present the case of inhomogeneous universe with perturbations 
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\rho(t, \mathbf{x}) - \bar{\rho}(t)}{\bar{\rho}(t)} << 1,
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\rho}(t)$ is the average density at a cosmic time $t$, and $\rho(t, \mathbf{x})$ is the density at a comoving position $\mathbf{x}$ and cosmic time $t$. In this thesis, we consider the $\Lambda$CDM cosmological model and describe its components in \ref{theory_sec:cosmo_model}.


\section{The cosmological model}\label{theory_sec:cosmo_model}
In this section, we consider the $\Lambda$CDM cosmological model and explain its components. As noted by Eqs. \ref{eq:Friedmann}, the time evolution of the scale factor $a$ depends on the density and its relation to the pressure, known as the equation of state. If we refer to the current day value of the Hubble parameter as $H_0$ and the current day value of the density $\rho_{0}$, Eq. \ref{eq:Friedmann2} can be written in the current epoch
\begin{equation}
    1 = \frac{8\pi G}{3H_0^2} \rho_0 + \frac{\Lambda c^2}{3H_0^2} - \frac{kc^2}{H_0^2} .
\end{equation}
Notice that the quantitiy $\frac{3H_0^2}{8\pi G}$ has the dimensionality of a density, and we call it the critical density $\rho_{cr}$. It is possible to express the density fractions of the different components in terms of the current day value of the component's density and the critical density.
\begin{subequations} \label{eq:Omega_params}
\begin{align}
    \Omega_0 &= \frac{\rho_0}{\rho_{cr}}  \label{eq:Omega_m}, \\
    \Omega_{\Lambda} &= \frac{\Lambda c^2}{3H_0^2} \label{eq:Omega_L}, \\
    \Omega_{k} &= -\frac{kc^2}{H_0^2} \label{eq:Omega_k}.
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
We list below the constituents of the $\Lambda$CDM model of cosmology. It is common to express the density of a constituent as a fraction of the critical density, such that the sum of these fractions equals 1. In practice, the density fraction of a constituent $X$ in the current epoch $\Omega_X$ is expressed as
\begin{equation}
    \Omega_X = \frac{\rho_{0, X}}{\rho_{cr}},
\end{equation}
with $\rho_{0, X}$ being the density of a constituent $X$ at the current epoch.

Measurements from the cosmic microwave background has shown that we live in a flat universe \citep{Smoot_1999}, meaning that $ \Omega_{k} = 0$ (within error bounds). \textbf{For the rest of this work, I assume a flat universe}. The two remaining components are the density fraction $\Omega_0$ and the fraction $\Omega_{\Lambda}$. The mechanism of how these components were formed into existence will be omitted from this thesis. It is worth noting that the Hubble parameter $H_0$ is conventionally given in term of a dimensionless number $h$ such that 
\begin{equation}
    H_0 = 100 \, h \, \mathrm{km} \, \mathrm{s}^{-1} \, \mathrm{Mpc}^{-1},
\end{equation}
and many other quantities are expressed in terms of $h$; for example, the density ratios $\Omega_X$ could be given as $\Omega_X h^2$. Measurements of the CMB with the Planck satellite \citep{aghanim2020planck} reported a result of 


\begin{equation*}
    H_0 = 67.66 \pm 0.42 \mathrm{km} \, \mathrm{s}^{-1} \, \mathrm{Mpc}^{-1},
\end{equation*}

meanwhile measurements using supernovae from the SH0ES collaboration \citep{riess2022comprehensive} report a value of

\begin{equation*}
    H_0 = 73.04 \pm 1.04 \mathrm{km} \, \mathrm{s}^{-1} \, \mathrm{Mpc}^{-1}.
\end{equation*}

 The discrepancy of $H_0$ measurements between the early universe with the CMB and late universe measurements with supernovae is called the Hubble tension \citep{verde2024tale}. 

The equation of state of each component in the $\Lambda$CDM model differs between the early universe with large energy scales and the late universe with very low energy scales. We list below each component and its role in the cosmic evolution of the universe. In general, the equation of state $p_X(\rho_X)$ expresses the relationship between pressure $p_X$ and density $\rho_X$ of a constituent $X$. Motivated by dimensional analysis \footnote{Dimensional analysis is the method of comparing the units of two physical quantities to conclude the relationship between them up to a proportionality constant.}, we can conclude that the pressure and density are related
\begin{equation}
    p_X = w \rho_X c^2. 
\end{equation}

The relationship between the scale factor $a$ and the density $\rho_X$ could be found by invoking the first law of thermodynamics and solving the following ordinary differential equation,
\begin{equation} \label{eq:adiabatic}
    \mathrm{d}(\rho_X c^2 a^3) = -p_X \, \mathrm{d}(a^3) .
\end{equation}

In total, the $\Lambda$CDM universe contains the following constituents:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Radiation:} Radiation is mostly composed of freely propagating relativistic particles: photons and neutrinos. Photons have been propagating in the universe since recombination. Recombination is the epoch when free electrons combine with nuclei to form neutral atoms, after which the photon mean free path becomes effectively infinite and photons decouple from matter; these photons are observed today as the cosmic microwave background (CMB) radiation. CMB anisotropies contain a wealth of information about the density fractions of the other components of the Universe and its spatial curvature. The temperature of the photon background scales as $T_{r} \propto a^{-1}$. With equation of state
    \begin{equation}
        p_r = \frac{1}{3} \rho_{r} c^2,
    \end{equation}
    and the radiation energy density scales as $\rho_{r} \propto a^{-4}$.
    \begin{equation}
        \rho_{r} = \rho_{r,0} a^{-4}.
    \end{equation}
    In addition to photon, neutrinos contribute to the radiation budget in the universe. At early times, neutrinos are in equilibrium with other particles such as electrons and photons, however, as the universe expands and the temperature drops, electrons no longer interact with neutrinos, therefore, neutrinos decouple from the rest of the components in the universe in a process known as freeze-out. The density of neutrinos scales to the scale factor in a similar way to photons, however, the present day temperature of neutrinos is different from that of photons  


    \item \textbf{Baryonic matter:} Baryonic matter is composed of the particles that make up the standard model of particle physics. The particles that make up this component interact with light and with itself through long range pressure. In the early universe, baryonic matter is coupled to photons through absorption, emission, and scattering processes. Due to the expansion of the universe, the absorption cross-section approaches zero, therefore, the photons emitted from recombining electrons with nuclei have less energy than needed to ionize a similar atom, leaving the photon free. The process of forming neutral atoms by combining free electrons with nuclei is called recombination. This process continues until all the atoms are neutral, and then, photons propagate freely in the universe, with a very low probability of interacting with Baryons. After an epoch of structure formation (with the universe being transparent), neutral gas clouds in dark matter halos collapse under gravity to form stars and trigger fusion reactions \footnote{A fusion reaction is a nuclear reaction when two low mass atoms combine to form a heavier atom, emitting an amount of energy.}. Photons emitted from stars reionize the medium around stars, and baryons interact again with photons \citep{Draine_2011}. The fraction of baryonic matter in the universe is measured to be $ \Omega_{\mathrm{b}} h^2 = 0.0225 \pm 0.0003$. In the early universe, the equation of state of baryonic matter is derived with relativistic consideration, however, after recombination, the short range pressure of baryons is negligible and the equation of state for baryons is that of dark matter on large scales. On small scale, baryonic processes in the late universe are highly non-linear and cumbersome to model which impose a limitation on the modelling of the cosmic shear signal. \textbf{This is the exact problem that this thesis is trying to tackle}.
    \item \textbf{Dark matter:} Dark matter is matter that does not interact with light, however, its effect is observed gravitationally. Recent results from different cosmological probes including CMB, galaxy clustering and weak lensing favour a cold dark matter component, meaning that the temperature of dark matter is much less than its mass. As a consequence, dark matter could be modelled as a pressureless fluid on large scale (on small scales, the fluid approximation breaks down). Using this simple picture of cold dark matter (CDM), it is possible to solve the fluid equation of dark matter to linear order and obtain a good approximation of the linear density evolution with cosmic time (I show this derivation below). The equation of state of dark matter, therefore, is simply $p = 0$. By solving Eq. \ref{eq:adiabatic} for dark matter, the density of dark matter depends on the scale factor $a$ such that
    \begin{equation}
        \rho_c = \rho_{c, 0} a^{-3}.
    \end{equation}
    The present day value of the dark matter density fraction is measured to be $\Omega_{\mathrm{m}} h^2 = 0.1431 \pm 0.0025$ by the Planck collaboration \citep{aghanim2020planck}. Dark matter is the main driver of matter clustering in the universe since dark matter can form virialized region of space called dark matter halos which accretes baryons and leads to galaxy formation as a consequence \citep{Wechsler_Tinker_2018}. 
    \item \textbf{Dark energy:} dark energy is a term coined by Michael S. Turner \citep{Turner_2001} to describe the contribution to the energy budget that is not in a form of particles. From Eqs. \ref{eq:Omega_params}, dark energy is the component with density fraction $\Omega_\Lambda$. Early on, this component was considered to be smooth and homogeneous, meaning that its corresponding density $\rho_\Lambda = \Omega_\Lambda \rho_{cr}$ only varies with cosmic time. In its simplest form, the dark energy pressure is related to the dark energy density as follows
    \begin{equation}
        p_\Lambda = w_\Lambda \rho_\Lambda c^2,
    \end{equation}
    with the simplest case $w_\Lambda = -1$. The equation of state of dark energy is highly debated with many modification suggested to explain tensions in cosmology such as the Hubble tension \citep{verde2024tale}. In this work, we assume an equation of state of dark energy with $w_\Lambda = -1$. The Planck collaboration \citep{aghanim2020planck} reported that $\Omega_\Lambda = 0.6889 \pm 0.0056$.
\end{itemize}

Figure \ref{theory_fig:energy_budget} shows a schematic representation of the components discussed above. In general, the matter density fraction $\Omega_{\mathrm{m}}$ is the sum of the baryonic matter density fraction $\Omega_{\mathrm{b}}$ and the dark matter density fraction $\Omega_c$. The total energy budget of the universe is the sum of the matter density fraction $\Omega_{\mathrm{m}}$, the dark energy density fraction $\Omega_\Lambda$, and the radiation density fraction $\Omega_r$. Since the radiation density fraction is negligible in the current epoch, we have $\Omega_{\mathrm{m}} + \Omega_\Lambda \approx 1$. With that, the evolution of the expansion rate of the universe in terms of the scale factor $a$ is given by
\begin{equation}
    H^2 (a) = H_0^2 \left[ \Omega_r a^{-4} + \Omega_{\mathrm{m}} a^{-3} + \Omega_\Lambda \right], 
\end{equation}
where the different additive terms dominate the evolution at different epochs. In particular, dark energy is the dominant component in the current epoch. To constrain the equation of state of dark energy, cosmic shear stands as a powerful probe of late structure formation since it is an integrated qunatity that includes signficant contribution from the late evolution of the universe, and therefore sensitive to dark energy. The basic principles of cosmic shear and its relationship to the matter density field are explained in \ref{theory_sec:WL}. This thesis focuses on using FRBs to constain baryonic feedback on the large scale structure and therefore improve the modelling of cosmic shear on small scales, which in turn improves the constraining power of cosmic shear on dark energy.

So far, I have only introduced the universe as a homogeneous entity, ignoring the fact the universe is inhomogeneous on small scales. As a physical system, the initial conditions of the universe are scale-dependent Gaussian perturbations which grow to be highly non-linear (and non-Gaussian) large perturbations (large relative to the mean density of the universe). Since the initial perturbations were generated via a stochastic process, the resultant perturbations are called random fields. In particular, the matter density field $\rho_{\mathrm{m}} (\mathbf{x}, t)$ is the random field that describes the matter density at a comoving position $\mathbf{x}$ and cosmic time $t$. For simplicity, the matter perturbations are often expressed in terms of the matter density contrast $\delta_{\mathrm{m}}$ such that
\begin{equation}
    \delta_{\mathrm{m}} (\mathbf{x}, t) = \frac{\rho_{\mathrm{m}} (\mathbf{x}, t) - \bar{\rho}_{\mathrm{m}}(t)}{\bar{\rho}_{\mathrm{m}}(t)},
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\rho}_{\mathrm{m}}(t)$ is the average matter density at a cosmic time $t$. In addition, similar definitions apply to any other density field such as the baryonic density contrast or the electron density contrast, which will be important in the study of FRBs. By construction, any density contrast field is a random field with a mean of zero i.e. $\langle \delta_{\mathrm{m}} \rangle = 0$. Higher order statistics of a random field also encode information about the random field. This thesis only uses the two-point statistics of the matter density contrast and its Fourier space counterpart, namely the power spectrum. We discuss these statistics below. Since dark matter is the dominant component of matter in the universe, it is the component responsible for the observed structure in the universe. In addition, the pressure induced by baryonic processes is negligible on large scales, meaning that the evolution of matter density contrast on large scales is well approximated by the evolution of dark matter density contrast. Therefore, the evolution of matter density contrast $\delta_{\mathrm{m}}$ is well approximated by the evolution of dark matter density contrast $\delta_c$. I present below the Einstein-Boltzmann equation that describes the phase-space evolution of a particle species in the universe.



\section{The Boltzmann equation \& the linear perturbation theory} \label{}
This section is partly based on a review on the effective field theory of large scale structure (EFTofLSS) by Tobias Baldauf \citep{Baldauf_EFTLSS_2020}. The Boltzmann equation describes the evolution of the phase-space distribution function of a particle species in the universe. This function is labelled as $f(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{p}, t)$, where $\mathbf{x}$ is the position, $\mathbf{p}$ is the momentum, and $t$ is the cosmic time. Different observables could be obtained from the distribution function $f$ including the density $\rho(\mathbf{x}, t)$ and the velocity field $\mathbf{v}(\mathbf{x}, t)$. The Boltzmann equation for a collisionless and pressureless dark matter

\begin{equation} \label{eq:Boltzmann}
\begin{split}
    \frac{\mathrm{d}f}{\mathrm{d}t} &= \frac{\partial f}{\partial t} + \frac{\mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}}{\mathrm{d}t} \cdot \frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{x}} + \frac{\mathrm{d}\mathbf{p}}{\mathrm{d}t} \frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{p}}  \\
    &= \frac{\partial f}{\partial t} + \frac{\mathbf{p}}{ma} \cdot \frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{x}} - am\nabla\phi \cdot \frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{p}} = 0,
\end{split}
\end{equation}
with $m$ being the mass of the dark matter particle. If dark matter behaves as a fluid, the density and the bulk velocity of the fluid could be obtained by taking the first two moments of the distribution function $f$ such that 
\begin{align} 
    \rho_{\mathrm{m}}(\mathbf{x}, t) &= \frac{m}{a^3} \int \mathrm{d}^3p \, f(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{p}, t), \label{eq:rho} \\
    v_i(\mathbf{x}, t) &= \int \mathrm{d}^3p \, \frac{p_i}{am} f(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{p}, t) \Bigg/ \int \mathrm{d}^3p \, f(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{p}, t), \label{eq:vi} \\
    \sigma_{ij}(\mathbf{x}, t) &= \int \mathrm{d}^3p \, \frac{p_i}{am} \frac{p_j}{am} f(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{p}, t) \Bigg/ \int \mathrm{d}^3p \, f(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{p}, t) - v_i(\mathbf{x}) v_j(\mathbf{x}), \label{eq:sigma}
\end{align}
with $\sigma_{ij}(\mathbf{x}, t)$ representing the velocity dispersion tensor. The distribution function $f$ has higher order moments, however, the anisotropic stress tensor is second order in momentum, therefore, it can be postulated that it is negligible at linear order and at early times where the velocity dispersion is small\footnote{The usual practice in standard perturbation theory is to truncate the Boltzmann hierarchy even for higher order perturbations}. In this thesis, I am only interested in the evolution of the matter density contrast. Using Eq. \ref{eq:Boltzmann}, the evolution of the matter density contrast and the bulk velocity are governed by the continuity and the Euler equations. The equations up to linear order are as follows
\begin{subequations} \label{eq:continuity_euler}
\begin{align}
    \frac{\partial \delta_{\mathrm{m}}}{\partial \tau} + \frac{1}{a} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v} & = 0, \\
    \frac{\partial \mathbf{v}}{\partial \tau} + \frac{1}{a} (\mathbf{v} \cdot \nabla) \mathbf{v} &= -\frac{1}{a} \nabla \phi,
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
with $\tau$ being the conformal time defined as $\mathrm{d}\tau = \mathrm{d}t/a$. Eqs. \ref{eq:continuity_euler} show that at a linear order the matter density contrast is coupled to the velocity field, and the velocity field is coupled to the gravitational field $\phi$. The growth of structure is, therefore, sourced by gravity. 

To solve the equations in real space is a cumbersome task, therefore, we take the following steps in order to obtain the evolution of the matter density contrast.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Apply the Fourier transform to Eqs. \ref{eq:continuity_euler} to obtain the equations in Fourier space. The operator $\nabla$ reduces to a multiplication by $i\mathbf{k}$, with $\mathbf{k}$ being the Fourier space wavevector.
    \item The velocity field is a vector field, therefore, it can be decomposed into a curl-free component and a divergence-free component. The curl-free component is called the velocity divergence $\theta$ and is defined as $\theta = \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}$, while the divergence-free component is called the vorticity $\omega$ and is defined as $\omega = \nabla \times \mathbf{v}$. At linear order, the vorticity decays with time, therefore, it can be neglected at linear order.
    \item The peculiar gravitational potential $\phi$ can be related to the matter density contrast by the relationship known as the Poisson equation such that
    \begin{equation}
    \nabla^2 \phi = \frac{3}{2}\Omega_{\mathrm{m}}H_0^2\frac{\delta_{\mathrm{m}}}{a}.
    \end{equation}
\end{itemize}
With these steps, the equation that governs the eveolution of the matter denstiy field at a linear level is a second order ordinary differential equation with respect to the conformal time $\tau$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:}
    \frac{\mathrm{d}^2 \delta_{\mathrm{m}}}{\mathrm{d}\tau^2} + \mathcal{H} \frac{\mathrm{d} \delta_{\mathrm{m}}}{\mathrm{d}\tau} - \frac{3}{2}\Omega_{\mathrm{m}}H_0^2\frac{\delta_{\mathrm{m}}}{a} = 0,
\end{equation}with $\mathcal{H} = aH$ being the conformal Hubble parameter. A solution for the matter density contrast $\delta$\footnote{$\delta$ refers to the matter density contrast $\delta_{\mathrm{m}}$ hereafter, and the subsript is omitted for ease of writing.} at linear order in Fourier space as follows
\begin{equation}\label{eq:linear_solution}
    \delta(\mathbf{k}, t) = D_+(\tau)\delta_{+,0}(\mathbf{k}),
\end{equation}
where $\delta_{+,0}$ is the initial conditions of the matter density contrast, and $D_+(\tau)$ is the growth factor whose exact expression depends on the cosmological model considered. For an Einstein-de Sitter universe, which is a universe with $\Omega_{\mathrm{m}} = 1$ and $\Omega_\Lambda = 0$, the growth factor is simply $D_+(\tau) = a(\tau)$. The importance of the result \ref{eq:linear_solution} lies in two observations: the first is that the growth of each Fourier mode of the matter density contrast is independent from other modes (modes of different length scales are not coupled), and the second is that the time evolution of the matter density contrast is separable from its spatial dependence. 

The solution to Eqs. \ref{eq:continuity_euler} is given assuming that the universe is way into matter dominated epoch, where the impact of radiation is negligible. In the early universe, the radiation component is dominant and initiates pressure that only acts on sacles smaller that the horizon scale, defined in Eq. \ref{eq:hubble_scale}, since pressure is a causal process that can only propagate at a speed less than the speed of light. Therefore, $\delta_{+,0}(\mathbf{k})$ should encode all the physics of the early universe including the impact of radiation on the matter density contrast and the initial conditions. In practice, comparing the evolved density field with observaiton will not give a conclusive answer on whether the theory presented here is correct or not, since the current universe is one realisaiton of the stochastic process that generated the initial conditions, therefore, estimating the statistical properties of the observed matter density field and comparing it with the theoretical prediction is the way to test the theory. In the next section, I present the power spectrum as a statistic that encodes the statistical properties of the matter density field.

At early times, the amplitude of the matter density contrast is small, therefore, the linear solution in Eq. \ref{eq:linear_solution} is a good approximation to the evolution of the matter density contrast. As time goes on, the amplitude of the matter density contrast grows and the linear approximation breaks down. Furthermore, for regions in the universe where the average density within this specfic region is large compared to the average density of the universe, dark matter paricles collapse under the influence of gravity and virialised regions of space form. These virialised regions are called dark matter halos.  The appendix include an explanation of the toy model for the collapse. {\color{red} add the number of the appendix}. Baryons fall in the deep potential well of the dark matter halo and accretes to form galaxies, meaning that galaxies traces the position of dark matter halos, and in turn dark matter.


\section{The 2-point correlation function and the power spectrum} \label{theory_sec:statistics}
Since $\delta_{0}(\mathbf{k})$ is a Gaussian random field, its statistical properties are completely determined by its mean and variance. Starting from $\delta_{0}(\mathbf{k})$, the matter density contrast in real space reads
\begin{equation} \label{eq:fourier}
    \delta(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int \delta(\mathbf{k}) e^{i\mathbf{k}.\mathbf{x}} \mathrm{d} \mathbf{k}.
\end{equation}
The mean of $\delta_{0}(\mathbf{k})$ is zero by construction, therefore, the variance is the only quantity that encodes the statistical properties of $\delta_{0}(\mathbf{k})$. The variance of $\delta_{0}(\mathbf{k})$ is called the power spectrum and is defined as
\begin{equation} \label{eq:power_spectrum}
    \langle \delta_{0}(\mathbf{k}) \delta_{0}^*(\mathbf{k}') \rangle = (2\pi)^3 P(k) \delta_D(\mathbf{k} - \mathbf{k}'),
\end{equation}
where $\delta_D$ is the Dirac delta function. Notice that the power spectrum is only a function of the magnitude of the Fourier space wavevector $k = |\mathbf{k}|$ due to the statistical isotropy of the universe. Fig. \ref{theory_fig:power_spectrum_varying_params} shows the matter power spectrum obtain using the CLASS Boltzmann solver \citep{lesgourgues2011cosmic} with varying fraction of baryonic matter $\Omega_{\mathrm{b}}$ and varying Hubble parameter $H_0$ at redshift $z = 0$.

The real space counterpart of the power spectrum is called the 2-point correlation function $\xi(r)$, and is defined assume
\begin{equation}
    \xi(r) = \langle \delta(\mathbf{x}) \delta(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{r})\rangle
\end{equation}
where $\xi$ is a function of the comoving lenth scale $r$ and is independent of direction. In practice, $\xi$ represent the excess probability that two points separated with $r$ have denisty contrast of $\delta(\mathbf{x})$ and  $\delta(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{r})$ respectively.

Though the matter power spectrum is a powerful statistic that encodes the statistical properties of the matter density field, it is not directly observable. In order to relate the matter field to obervables, it is important to understand the physical mechanisms by which the observables are related to the matter field. For galaxies, galaxy formation theories suggest that baryonic matter falls into the potential well of a dark matter halo and accretes to form a galaxy. The detailed mechanism of how this happens is highly non-linear and complicated. In addition, running comprehensive galaxy formation simulations is computationally expernsive in order to cover the large parameter space of galaxy formation physics \citep{somerville2015physical}. Therefore, it is common to use a phenomenological model to relate the galaxy field to the matter field. The simplest model is the linear bias model which assumes that the galaxy number density contrast $\delta_g$ is linearly related to the matter density contrast $\delta$ such that
\begin{equation}
    \delta_g (\mathbf{x}, t) = b(t) \delta(\mathbf{x}, t),
\end{equation}
with $b$ being the linear bias parameter. The linear bias model is a good approximation on large scales, however, on small scales, the relationship between the galaxy field and the matter field is highly non-linear and the linear bias model breaks down \citep{desjacques2018large}. Notice that the galaxy number density contrast is only dependent on the local matter density field and does not depend on the environment surrounding the galaxy. This approximation breaks down on small scales where th environment of a galaxy can have a significant impact of the galaxy formation process. Galaxies are called 'biased tracers of dark matter' since they trace the underlying matter field. For any tracer $\mathrm{u}$ of dark matter, I define the pwoer spectrum of the density constrast of this tracer $\delta_\mathrm{u}$ in a similar fashion to Eq. \ref{eq:power_spectrum}
\begin{equation} \label{eq:power_spectrum_tracers}
    \langle \delta_\mathrm{u}(\mathbf{k}) \delta_\mathrm{u}^*(\mathbf{k}') \rangle = (2\pi)^3 P_{uu}(k) \delta_D(\mathbf{k} - \mathbf{k}').
\end{equation}

It is possible to use the same formalism of galaxy bias to relate the matter density contrast with other tracers of dark matter such as dark matter halos. In practice, the bias of dark matter halos is related to the underlzing matter field via the following relationship
\begin{equation}\label{eq:halo_bias}
    \delta_h (\mathbf{x}, t) = b_h(t) \delta(\mathbf{x}, t),
\end{equation}
with $b_h$ being the dark matter halo bias parameter. Owing to Eq. \ref{eq:halo_bias}, the halo power psectrum can be expressed in terms of the matter power spectrum defined in Eq. \ref{eq:power_spectrum} such that 
\begin{equation}
    P_{hh} (k, t) = b_{h}^2 (t) P_{mm} (k, t).
\end{equation}


For dark matter halos, it is possible to go bayond a linear bias model by fitting an empirical model of halo bias to the results of an N-body gravitional simulation. In this thesis, I use the fitting formula of \citet{tinker2010large} to model the halo bias. In this recipe, the halo bias is a function of the halo mass and its redshift $b_h (M, z)$. As a consequence, the halo power spectrum is related to the matter power spectrum with the relationship
\begin{equation} \label{eq:halo_spectrum}
    P_{\mathrm{hh}}(M_1, M_2, k) = b(M_1) b(M_2) P_{\mathrm{mm}}^{\mathrm{lin}}(k) [1 + \beta^{\mathrm{nl}}(M_1, M_2, k)].
\end{equation}

In the next section, I present the halo model, a non-pertubative approximation to obtain the non-linear power spectrum of matter or a tracer of matter. In particular, this thesis is concerned with the electron power spectrum in order to model the cosmological contribution of the dispersion measure of fast radio bursts.
\subsection{The angular power specrtum}
The definition of the power spectrum in Eq. \ref{eq:power_spectrum} assumes that the observed field is sampled in three-dimensional space, therefore, the density field in real space can be written in using the Fourier space exponent as in EQ. \ref{eq:fourier} which represent a full orthogonal basis. WHen observing cosmological fields on the sky, a different othogonal basis is needed to decompose the cosmological field since the values measured live on the unit spehre and not on a flat geometry. For this purpose, the a cosmological field $f$ in the direction $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ can be written in terms of the spherical harmonic functions $Y_{\boldsymbol{\ell}}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$, where $\boldsymbol{\ell}$ is the Fourier space counterpart of $\boldsymbol{\theta}$. Therefore, if $f_{\boldsymbol{\ell}}$ is the spherical harmonic coeficients and $\boldsymbol{\ell} = (\ell, m)$\footnote{more on the spherical harmonics can be found in the appendix} for a moment $m$ and a multipole $\ell$, the spherical harmonic transform and its inverse are given as
\begin{equation}
    f(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \sum_{\ell m} Y_{\ell m}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \, f_{\ell m} \quad \leftrightarrow \quad f_{\ell m} = \int \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{\theta} \, f(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \, Y_{\ell m}^*(\boldsymbol{\theta}).
\end{equation}

The angular power spectrum $C_\ell$ is then defined in terms of the spherical harmonic coefficients 
\begin{equation} \label{eq:ang_power_spectrum}
    \langle \hat{f}_{\ell m} \hat{f}_{\ell' m'}\rangle = \delta_{\ell \ell'} \delta_{m m'}C_{\ell},
\end{equation}
where $\delta$ here is the Kronecker delta. Notice how the power spectrum is not dependent on $m$ by virtue of the cosmological principle. 

The angular power specrtrum of a cosmological field can be obtained from the power spectrum of the same cosmological field or an integral of it. I show how to get the angular power spectrum from the electron power spectrum in \ref{theory_sec:FRBs}.
 


\section{The halo model \& the electron power spectrum} \label{theory_sec:halo_model}
The details in this section are based on a review of the halo model by \citet{asgari2023halo}. The halo model assumes that matter in the universe are entirely contained in dark matter halos. Therfore, any cosmological field $\delta_{\mathrm{u}}$ at a comoving coordinate $\mathbf{x}$ can be expressed in terms of the halo denisty profile $W_{\mathrm{u},i}$ such that \footnote{notice that the time dependency is omitted for ease of writing.}
\begin{equation}
    \delta_{\mathrm{u}}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i} N_i \, W_{\mathrm{u},i}\!\left(\left|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_i\right|\right),
\end{equation}
where the sum runs over all volume elements and $N_i = \{0, 1\}$ determines whether there is a halo centre in that volume element. $W_{\mathrm{u},i}$ could express the halo density profile for any comp onent that traces halos such as electrons. In N-body simulations, the halo profile $W_{\mathrm{u},i}$ of a particular dark matter halo $i$ difffers from one halo to another as a funciton of the halo mass $M$. To express this scatter, the halo-mass distribution function is introduced. The halo mass distrinution function $n(M)$ is defined as the number of dark matter halos with $M_i \in [M, M + \mathrm{d} M]$ is $n(M)\mathrm{d}M$. Averaging over all possible halo-masses, the expectation value of a field becomes

\begin{equation}
    \langle \delta_{\mathrm{u}}(\mathbf{x}) \rangle = \left\langle \sum_{i} N_i \, W_{\mathrm{u}}(M_i, |\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_i|) \right\rangle = \int_0^{\infty} \mathrm{d}M \, n(M) \int \mathrm{d}^3x' \, W_{\mathrm{u}}(M, |\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}'|) = \int_0^{\infty} \mathrm{d}M \, W_{\mathrm{u}}(M) n(M) \, ,
\end{equation}

where $W_\mathrm{u}(M, \mathbf{x})$ can be split into two multiplicative component: $W_u (M)$ which encodes the amplitud eof the halo profile and $U_\mathrm{u}(M, \mathbf{x})$ whichc encodes information about the shape of the profile, andd the mass-dependent halo profile can be written as
\begin{equation}
W_{\mathrm{u}}(M, x) = W_{\mathrm{u}}(M) U_{\mathrm{u}}(M, x),
\end{equation}
with $W_{\mathrm{u}}(M)$ appearing in the last equality, and $U_{\mathrm{u}}(M, x)$ is regourously defined as 
\begin{equation} \label{eq:halo_profile}
    \int \mathrm{d}^3x \, U_{\mathrm{u}}(M, x) = 1 \, .
\end{equation}
The physical intuition behind the function $W_{\mathrm{u}}(M, x)$ can be understood by going to Fourier space. First, Eq. \ref{eq:halo_profile} indicates that the Fourier space counterpart of the function $U_{\mathrm{u}}(M, x)$, namely $\hat{U}_{\mathrm{u}}(M, k)$ has the property $\hat{U}_{\mathrm{u}}(M, k \to 0) = 1$ which implies that $\hat{W}_{\mathrm{u}}(M, k \to 0) = \hat{W}_{\mathrm{u}}(M)$. THis result is understood that at large scales, dark matter halos act as point masses (similar to a Dirac-delta function) which translates to a constant in Fourier space.

With this formalism in place, the ingredients of the halo model allows an esitmation for the power spectrum of a species $\mathrm{u}$ (in the case of this thesis, the halo model formalism will be applied to obtain a prediction of the electron density in the universe). Using the definition of the pwoer spectrum in Eq. \ref{eq:power_spectrum}, the power spectrum of the field $\delta_u$ as described inthe halo model is
\begin{equation}
    \langle \hat{\delta}_{\mathrm{u}}(\mathbf{k}) \hat{\delta}_{\mathrm{u}}^*(\mathbf{k}') \rangle = \left\langle \sum_{i,j} e^{-i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{x}_i} \, e^{i\mathbf{k}'\cdot\mathbf{x}_j} \, N_i \, N_j \, \hat{W}_{\mathrm{u},i}(M,k) \, \hat{W}_{\mathrm{u},j}(M,k') \right\rangle.
\end{equation}
The sum can be further divided into two contributions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a contribution with $i = j$ which is the power restultant from the correlation of dark matter particles within one dark matter halo given by 
    \begin{equation} \label{eq:1h}
        P_{\mathrm{uu}}^{\mathrm{1h}}(k) = \int_0^{\infty} \hat{W}_{\mathrm{u}}(M,k) \, \hat{W}_{\mathrm{u}}(M,k) \, n(M) \, \mathrm{d}M \, ,
    \end{equation}
    and this contribution is termed as \textbf{the one-halo term}.
    \item a contrubution with $i \neq j$ whcih is the power resultant from the correlation of dark matter particles in two different halos given as
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:2h}
        P_{\mathrm{uu}}^{\mathrm{2h}}(k) = \int_0^{\infty} \int_0^{\infty} \mathrm{d}M_1 \, \mathrm{d}M_2 \, P_{\mathrm{hh}}(M_1, M_2, k) \, \hat{W}_{\mathrm{u}}(M_1, k) \, \hat{W}_{\mathrm{u}}(M_2, k) \, n(M_1) \, n(M_2),
    \end{equation}
    and this contribution is termed as \textbf{the two-halo contribution}. $P_{hh}$ is the halo power spectrum defined in Eq. \ref{eq:halo_spectrum}.
\end{itemize}
The non-linear power spetrum wihtin the halo model of a species $\mathrm{u}$ is given by 
\begin{equation}
    P_{uu}(k) =  P_{\mathrm{uu}}^{\mathrm{1h}}(k) + P_{\mathrm{uu}}^{\mathrm{2h}}(k).
\end{equation}
Fig. \ref{theory_fig:halo_model} shows an example of the non-linear power specrutm with the different contributions from the one-halo and the two-halo terms. Since $P_{\mathrm{uu}}^{\mathrm{1h}}$ describes the \textbf{inter-halo} clustering of a species $\mathrm{u}$, it dominates the power spctrum on small scales. Meanwhile,

However, for the use case specific to this thesis, the halo model is useful since it is able to model the small scales which are mostly impacted by baryoinic feedback accurately.


\section{Weak gravitational Lensing \& cosmic shear}\label{theory_sec:WL}
This section is based on the lecture notes of the \textit{Gravitational Lensing} cousrse taught by Prof. Dr. Peter Schneider at the University of Bonn.

Gravitational lensing, in the language of general relativity, is the modification of light (null) geodesics by virtue of the existance of massive objects. In the language of special relativity, it is the bening of light by virtute of the gravitional force of a massive object on a photon. As a result, the image of a bright object (galaxy or a quasar for example) gets magnified and sheared as compared to a situation where there are no lensing effects. In this thesis, I am only interested with the weak field limit of the gravitional lensing where only shear effects are observable. Fig. \ref{theory_fig:lensing} shows a schematic representation of shearing and magnification effects.

    \centering

    \caption{A schematic representation of the imapct of gravitational lensing on a source with a circular shape (blue). Lensing has two main effect: shearing (red) and magnification (green). In the weak field limit, the magnificaiton effects are negligible and shearing effects dominate.}
    \label{theory_fig:lensing}
\end{figure}



In the weak field limit, the shearing effects of gravitational lensing are dominant over the magnification effects. Since the matter density contrast in the present epoch is of order $\delta \approx 1$, it is possible to invoke the weak field approximation to study the shearing effects of distant galaxies' images by the large scale stucture. This effect is called \textbf{cosmic shear}. 




To understand the lensing of a galaxy image, the shape of a galaxy, or the ellipticity, is represented as a spin-2 field with two components\footnote{The image of a galaxy has an eliptical shape, therefore, it is possible to describe the shape with two principal components representing the minor axis and the major axis.} on the unit sphere. 

In this section, the symbole $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ is used to indicate a solid angle where $\boldsymbol{\theta} = \theta_1 + i\theta_2$ in the source plane. Fig. \ref{theory_fig:angle} shows how In practice,$\boldsymbol{\theta}$ is related to a displacement vector $\boldsymbol{\zeta}$ via the relation
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\zeta} = \chi \boldsymbol{\theta}.
\end{equation}
This is clear from Fig. \ref{theory_fig:angle}


\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{figs/lensing_angle.png}
    \caption{A schematic representation showing how the angle $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ is related }
    \label{theory_fig:angle}
\end{figure}

The source galaxy shape $\mathrm{\alpha}$ is observed with a lensed shape $\mathrm{\beta}$ via a transformation $\mathcal{A}$ such that
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{\beta} = \mathcal{A} \mathrm{\alpha},
\end{equation}
with $\mathcal{A}$ beging a $2 \times 2$ matrix. $\mathcal{A}$ can be written in terms of two components: the shear $\gamma$ and the convergence $\kappa$ such that
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 - \kappa - \gamma_1 & -\gamma_2 \\ -\gamma_2 & 1 - \kappa + \gamma_1 \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
where $\gamma = \gamma_1 + i \gamma_2$ in the the plain of complex numbers. Each of the convergence and the shear has both a geometric and a physical meaning. The trace of the $\mathcal{A}$ is $\text{tr}(\mathcal{A}) = 2(1 - \kappa)$, meaning that the magnification effects are controlled by the quatity $\kappa$. Meanwhile, $\gamma$ is the qunatity that controls the shearing of an image. In this formalism, THe convergence $\kappa$ is actullay a field, since it is related to the matter denstiy contrast $\delta$ via the relationship 
\begin{align}
\kappa(\boldsymbol{\theta}) &= \int \mathrm{d}\chi \, p_\chi(\chi) \, \kappa(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \chi) \notag \\
&= \frac{3H_0^2 \Omega_{\mathrm{m}}}{2c^2} \int_0^{\chi_{\mathrm{h}}} \mathrm{d}\chi \, g(\chi) \, f_K(\chi) \, \frac{\delta\!\left(f_K(\chi)\boldsymbol{\theta}, \chi\right)}{a(\chi)} \, ,
\end{align}
where $\chi$ is the comoving distance, $f_K (\chi) = \chi$ for a flat universe, and $g(\chi)$ is a geometric factor. Furthermore, $p_\chi(\chi)$ is the source distribution i.e. the probability of findingg a galaxy at cosmoving distance $\chi$.

The shear in turn, is a spin-2 field which is related to the convergence via the relationship
\begin{align} \label{eq:gamma_kappa}
\gamma(\boldsymbol{\theta}) &= \frac{1}{\pi} \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \mathrm{d}^2\theta' \, \mathcal{D}(\boldsymbol{\theta} - \boldsymbol{\theta}') \, \kappa(\boldsymbol{\theta}') \, , \quad \text{with} \notag \\
\mathcal{D}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) &\equiv -\frac{\theta_1^2 - \theta_2^2 + 2\mathrm{i}\theta_1\theta_2}{|\boldsymbol{\theta}|^4} = \frac{-1}{(\theta_1 - \mathrm{i}\theta_2)^2} \, .
\end{align}


As can be seen from Eq. \ref{eq:gamma_kappa}, the shear is a convolution of $\kappa$ with the kernel $\mathcal{D}$, or in other words $\mathcal{D}$ is the shear generated by a point mass (if $\kappa$ is a delta function then the shear is equal to $\mathcal{D}$ after the integral). This relation can be inverted to find that

\begin{align}
\kappa(\boldsymbol{\theta}) - \kappa_0 &= \frac{1}{\pi} \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \mathrm{d}^2\theta' \, \mathcal{D}^*(\boldsymbol{\theta} - \boldsymbol{\theta}') \, \gamma(\boldsymbol{\theta}') \notag \\
&= \frac{1}{\pi} \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \mathrm{d}^2\theta' \, \mathcal{R}\mathrm{e} \left[ \mathcal{D}^*(\boldsymbol{\theta} - \boldsymbol{\theta}') \, \gamma(\boldsymbol{\theta}') \right] \, ,
\end{align}
where $\kappa_0$ is a constant that arises since $\mathcal{D}$ is not defined at $\boldsymbol{\theta} = 0$. In simple words, shear and convergence are complementary quantities. Since the convergence is related to the matter density along a certain direction $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, so as the shear. Therefore, if the impact of shearing on galaxy images is studied, it is possible to constrain the matter density in the universe.

To relate the shear to the shape of galaxy there is one important assumptions. Galaxies in the universe are randomly oriented, however by virtue of the cosmological principles, the expectation value of galaxy shapes should be zero. If $\epsilon_s$ is the intrinsic shape of galaxy, therefore
\begin{equation}
    \langle \epsilon_s \rangle = 0.
\end{equation}
However, the observed shape of a galaxy $\epsilon$ is subject to lensing effects. In the weak field limit, the expectation value of the observed galaxy shapes is an unbiased estimator of the shear, meaning 
\begin{equation} \label{eq:shape_shear}
    \langle \epsilon \rangle = \gamma.
\end{equation}
This is an extremely important result which signals the possibility of constraining the matter distribution in the universe via cosmic shear. To use cosmic shear to infere the cosmological parameter, the relationship between the matter power spectrum and the convegence power spectrum is used. This relation is given in the Limber approximation 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:convergence_ps}
    P_{\kappa \kappa} (\ell) = \frac{9H_0^4 \Omega_{\mathrm{m}}^2}{4c^4} \int_0^{\chi_{\mathrm{h}}} \mathrm{d}\chi \, \frac{g^2(\chi)}{a^2(\chi)} \, P \!\left(\frac{\ell}{f_K(\chi)}, \chi\right),
\end{equation}
where $P = P(k, \chi)$ is the matter power spectrum at scale $k$ and cosmocving distance $\chi$. Furthermore, the shear power spectrum and the convergence power spectrum are the same, and by virture of Eq. \ref{eq:shape_shear}, the same as the galaxy shapes power spectrum,
\begin{equation}
    P_{\kappa \kappa} = P_{\gamma \gamma} = P_{\epsilon \epsilon}.
\end{equation}

Eq. \ref{eq:convergence_ps} shows that any approximation to obtain a theoretical prediction of the matter power spectrum $P(k, \chi)$ will also influence the shear power spectrum $P_{\gamma \gamma}$. One of the main assumptions in this calculation is that structure growth in the late universe is only gravitionally driven. However, baryonic feedback on the large scales structure can influence the distributino of matter in the unvierse on different scales \citep{van2011effects}. In section \ref{theory_sec:BF}, I discuss how baryonic feedback influences the power spectrum. 


\section{The impact of baryonic feedback on the large-scale structure}\label{theory_sec:BF}
This section is a qualitative discussion of baryonic feedback on the large sacles structure. It is mainly based on \citet{van2011effects}, one of the first papers to compare and contrast the impact of baryonic feedback on the matter power spectrum. Baryonic feedback are the set of baryonic physics processes that impact the distribution of matter \citep{Rudd_Zentner_Kravtsov_2008}. The real impact of these processes on the matte distributino in the universe is realised by analyzing hydrodynamical simulations. I list below of the feedback mechanism.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Active galactic nuclei (AGN) feedback:} AGN feedback originates when gas accretes on a black hole at the center of galaxy and falls into the black hole \citep{ishibashi2012active}. The blackhole then injects energy back into the gas that is accreting, warming up the gas and results in hot jets that can be observed up to scales of $\sim 1 \text{Mpc}$. When gas gets pushed ouside the perimeter of a galaxy, it creates a potential well that matter follows, reducing the clustering of matter inside a galaxy. Therefore, the impact of AGN feedback is manifested as supression of power at scales of order $k \sim 10 \text{h.Mpc}^{-1}$ compared to a scenario where only gravity influences structure fomation in the unvierse \citep{martin2025stirring}. 
    \item \textbf{Supernova feedback:} supernova feedback is a sudden collapse large-mass stars under the influence of gravity, leading to the release of energies in form of photons and charged particles on the order of $\sim 10^{51} \text{erg}$\footnote{1 Joul = $10^7$ erg} \citep{huang2020impact}. The energy reeleased warms up the gas within a galaxy and and enriches the interstellar medium and the circumgalactic medium with heavy metals that formed throughout the life time of the star. The exact impact on the matter power spectrum is not known a priori since each scenario of supernova feedback leads to different results and affect a range of (small) scales, and there is not reason to believe one scenario over the other. Nonetheles, by studying the different possible scenario, the supernova feedback on cosmological scales leads to $\sim 1-10 \% $ suppression in the matter power spectrum on scales between $k \sim 1-10 \text{h.Mpc}^{-1}$ \citep{van2011effects}.
    \item \text{Star formation feedback:} Due to multiple cooling mechanisms the gas in galaxy collapses under the influence of gravity to form stars. Star formation leads to increase of power up to $\sim 10\%$ on scales $k \gtrsim 10 \text{h.Mpc}^{-1}$ \citep{van2011effects}.
\end{itemize}

Fig. \ref{theory_fig:baryonic_feedback} shows a plot comparing the ratio of the power spectrum of dark matter only scenario $P^{\text{DMO}}_{\delta}$ to the matter power spectrum in different hydrodynamical simualtions $P^{\text{hydro}}_{\delta}$. Each of the given hydrodynamical simulations has different implmentation of small scale physics and is parameterized by a set of nuisance paremters of no particular cosmological. Using astrophysical observations, it is not possible to probe one of these scenarios over the other. Therefore, to mitigate the impact of baryonic feedback on the large scale structure, multiple statistical tools have been introduced, as discussed in the introduction. Other analytical approches involved parametrizing baryonic feedback with analytical models calibrated on hydrodynamical simulations \citep{mead2021hmcode, Schneider_Teyssier_2015}. These models have two shortcomings. First, the hydrodynamical simulation used for calibration is not guarenteed to agree with the true scenario of baryonic feedback in the universe. Second, the parameters that goven baryonic feedback have uniform and wide prior range, meaning that the constraining power of cosmological data will be absorbed by these nuisance parameters, leaving the cosmological parameters with no constraining power \citep{Schneider_Kovač_etal._2025}.

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{figs/baryonic_feedback.png}
    \caption{A plot adopted from \citet{huang2019modelling} showing the supression of the power spcctrum for different hydrodynamical simulations with different models for baryonic feedback. For simulations where supernova feedback and AGN feedback are present, the power is superessed on the scales where the gas pressure from these two feedback mechanism influences matter distribution. The ratio rises for larger for smaller scales since gas cooling leads to star formation, boosting the power on small scales.}
    \label{theory_fig:baryonic_feedback}
\end{figure}

To obtain tight priors on the baryonic feedback parameter, it is possible to use probes of gas distribution in the Universe which in turns traces the baryonic matter distribution. This can be done by observing the kinematic Sunyaev–Zeldovich (kSZ) effect from CMB maps \citep{sunyaev1972observations, liu2025measurements} or the X-ray observation of galaxy clusters . the kSZ is the inverse scattering of CMB photons when interracting with free energetic free electrons due to their bulk motion. This effect is observed as a secondary anisotropy in the observed maps of CMB. It is possible to relate the observed anisotropy $\Delta T_{\mathrm{kSZ}}$ due to kSZ with the electron number density along the line of sight 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kSZ}
    \frac{\Delta T_{\mathrm{kSZ}}}{T_{\mathrm{CMB}}} = \frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{T}}}{c} \int_{\mathrm{los}} \mathrm{d}l \, e^{-\tau} n_{\mathrm{e}} \, v_{\mathrm{p}},
\end{equation}
where $T_{\mathrm{CMB}} = 2.725 \text{K}$ the blackbody temprature of the CMB, $\sigma_{\mathrm{T}}$ the Thomson cross-section, $\tau$ the optical depth, $l$ is the co-moving distance along the line of sight, and $v_{\mathrm{p}}$ is the peculiar velocity of gas. Eq. \ref{eq:kSZ} indicates that it is possible to proble the distribution of baryons in the universe by extracting the secondary anisotropies in the CMB that are caused by the kSZ effect. Combining the measurements of kSZ with constraints on the hot gas mass fraction from X-ray observations of galaxy clusters can impose constraints constraints on the baryonic feedback parameters. These constraints can be used as priors in a comsological analysis of cosmic shear data yielding tighter constraints on the comsological parameter as compared to having wide priors on these paraneters. 

Though kSZ is a promising tool, extracting its signal from CMB observation requires assumptions about the temprature and ionization of the gas in galaxy clusters and the bulk velocity of the clusters \citep{carlstrom2002cosmology}. Thankfully, the measured DM of FRBs is a direct probe of the electron number denisty in the unvierse and thereofre baryonic feedback. In section \ref{theory_sec:FRBs}, I describe the use of FRBs in cosmology in addition to the systematic and measurement uncertainties which impacts the modelling and measurements of the DM signal.





\section{Fast radio bursts}\label{theory_sec:FRBs}
This section is based on \citet{alonso2021linear}, \citet{10.1093/mnras/stad1645} and \citet{Petroff_2019}. For ease of writing, I consider that $\hbar = c = 1$ in this section. As mentioned in the introduction, the DM of FRBs is proportional to the difference in arriveal time between high frequency wave and small frequency wave, and I show how this relationship rises below. Since the FRBs passes through the intergalactic medium (IGM), it interacts with the plasma in this medium. The plasma is made up out of charged electrons. Therefore, when a light wave of frequency $\omega$ and wave vector $\mathbf{k}$, the plasma follows the dispersion relation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dispersion}
    \omega^2 - |\mathbf{k}|^2 = \omega_e^2, \quad \omega_e \equiv \frac{4\pi e^2 n_\mathrm{e}}{m_e},
\end{equation}
where $n_\mathrm{e}$ is the electron number density as measured by the plasma's frame. The velocity of the electromagnetic wave packet in the plasma's frame of reference $v_g$, is equivalent to the velocity of a massive particle with mass $\omega_e$ and energy $\omega$
\begin{equation}
    v_g = \sqrt{1 - \frac{\omega_e^2}{\omega^2}}.
\end{equation}
Since the FRBs are in the radio regime, the ratio $\frac{\omega_\mathrm{e}^2}{\omega^2} << 1$, and the group velocity can be approximated assume
\begin{equation}
    v_g \approx \left(1 - \frac{1}{2}\frac{\omega_\mathrm{e}^2}{\omega^2}\right).
\end{equation}
To calculate the time delay due to scattering of the free electrons, I turn the problem into calculating the time daley between the arrival of a pulse with frequency $\nu_0$ and a pulse of infinite frequency. I call these pulses a normal and infinite pulses respectively. Once I have an expression for the time delay, it possible to calculate the time delay between pulses of a high frequency $\nu_{\text{high}}$ and a low frequency $\nu_{\text{low}}$ Theoretically, plasma does not affect the infinite pulse since the pulse has infinite momentum. Therefore, the group velocity of the infinite pulse is $1$.
I use the setup in Fig. \ref{theory_fig:time_delay} to find an expression of the imte delay. The setup shows freely falling frame of references along the trajectory of the normal pulse. The time delay beetween the arrival of the normal pulse and the arrival of the infinite pulse (the two events are marked by an \textbf{x} on the yellow lines) can be given by 
\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{figs/time_delay.png}
    \caption{Spacetime diagram showing the evaluation of time dalay between a pulse of frequency $\nu_0$ (world line in red) and a pulse of infinite frequency (world lines in blue and dashed). The plasma particles follow world lines given in yellow color. The figure is adopted from \citet{alonso2021linear}}.
    \label{theory_fig:time_delay}
\end{figure}
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{d} \tau = \frac{\mathrm{d} l}{v_g} - \mathrm{d} l,
\end{equation}
with $\tau$ being the proper time measured by the plasma (notice that I used the group velocity relative to the plasma) and $\mathrm{d} l$ the proper ditsnce between the two stations. The correponding time delay measured by the comoving observer $\mathrm{d} t$ is given by
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{d}t = (1 + z)\mathrm{d}\tau.
\end{equation}
If the normal pulse has a 4-momentum defined as $P^\mu = \mathrm{d}x^\mu / \mathrm{d}\Lambda$ (see Eq. \ref{eq:4-velocity}), where $\lambda$ is an affine parameter, the proper length can be written as $\mathrm{d} l = P^\mu u_{e, \mu}$, where $u_{e, \mu}$ is the station's 4-velocity as measured by the observed. Thus to lowest order in $\omega_\mathrm{e} / \omega$, the measured time $\mathrm{d}t$ is written
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{d}t = \frac{(1+z)\omega_\mathrm{e}^2}{2\omega^2} \left( P^\mu u_{e,\mu} \right) \mathrm{d}\lambda.
\end{equation}
Finally, due to cosmological redshift, the station observes a frequency $\omega$ different from the what the observers detects, as shown by Eq. \ref{eq:redshift_freq}. We relate the two frequencies via the relation $\omega = 2\pi \nu_0 (1 + z)$, making the final expression for the time delay
\begin{equation}
    \Delta t = \frac{e^2}{2\pi m_\mathrm{e} \nu_o^2} \mathrm{DM},
\end{equation}
where DM is the dispersion measure given as an integral
\begin{equation} \label{eq:dispersion_full}
    \mathrm{DM} \equiv \int_{\lambda_\mathrm{e}}^{\lambda_o} \mathrm{d}\lambda \left( n_\mathrm{e} \frac{P^\mu u_{e,\mu}}{1+z} \right)\Bigg|_{\lambda},
\end{equation}
where the subscript $\lambda$ is a reminder tha tthe integrand is evaluated along the pulse's trajectory. In a realistic situation, the plasma has a peculiar velocity and the normal pulse does not follow a null geodesic. I consider a situation where the stations are comoving observer with 4-velocity $u_{e}^\mu = (1, 0, 0, 0)$, and the pulse moves along a null geodesic in an unperturbed universe with momentum $P^\mu = (A/a, A/a^2, 0, 0)$\footnote{I omit the proof to obtain this results. The proof can be done by solving the geodesic equation (Eq. \ref{eq:geodesic}) and setting the coordinate origin such that the photon follows a geodesic with $\phi = 0$ and $\theta =0$.}, where $A$ is a constant. The inner producet of momentum and veloctiy reduces to 
\begin{equation}
    P^\mu u_{e,\mu} = g_{tt}(A/a)(1)  = -A/a.
\end{equation}
Since $\lambda$ parametrizes the geodesic of the pulse, it is possible to change variables in Eq. \ref{eq:dispersion_full} using the expression for the 4-momentum where $\mathrm{d}t/\mathrm{d}\lambda = A/a$. The integral becomes
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{DM} \equiv \int_{t_o}^{t_e} \mathrm{d}t \, a \, n_\mathrm{e},
\end{equation}
where I have changed the integral pounds. Remeber that $n_\mathrm{e}$ is the physical density as measured by the plasma. It can be written in terms of the comoving density as $n_\mathrm{e} = n_{\mathrm{e}, c} a^{-3}$. Therefore, the correpoinding comoving density scales with Using the relationship between cosmic time $t$ and the sacle factor $a$ as in Eq. \ref{eq:hubble} and the relationship between the $a$ and $z$ in Eq. \ref{eq:redshift}, the integral can be written as
\begin{equation} \label{eq:dispersion_ne}
    \mathrm{DM} \equiv \int^{z_e}_{0} \mathrm{d}z' \frac{1 + z'}{H(z')}  n_{\mathrm{e}, c}(z').
\end{equation}
Hereafter, I work with comiving qunatities and will label the comoving electorn density at a redshift $z$ with $n_\mathrm{e}$. Eq. \ref{eq:dispersion_ne} is the dispersion measure-redshift relation $\mathrm{DM}_z$ which I use in this thesis to study the impact of systematic uncertainties. Eq. \ref{eq:dispersion} includes an expression of the comoving electron number density $n_{\mathrm{e}, c}$ this quantity can be written in terms of the baryon density fraciton $\Omega_\mathrm{b}$ given some consideration to the ratio of free electron in the universe that disperses the signal. In general, the expression should look like
\begin{equation}
    n_{\mathrm{e}} = \frac{\bar{\rho}_\mathrm{b}}{m_\mathrm{p}}\, F(z),
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\rho}_\mathrm{b}$ is the average of the baryonic density in the universe, $m_\mathrm{p}$ is the mass of proton, and $F(z)$ is the fraciotn of free electrons in the intergalactic medium. To find $F(z)$, I make the following considerations
\begin{itemize}
    \item If the universe has only helium and hydrogen atom, then the fraction of free electrons in the universe is $\chi_\mathrm{e} = Y_{\mathrm{H}}X_{\mathrm{e, H}}(z) + \frac{1}{2}Y_{\mathrm{H}} X_{\mathrm{e, He}}(z)$, with $Y_{\mathrm{H}}$,  $Y_{\mathrm{He}}$, $X_{\mathrm{e, H}}(z)$, and $X_{\mathrm{e, He}}(z)$ the mass fraction of hydrogen, the mass fraction of helium, the ionized fraction of hydrogen, and the ionized fraction of helium respectively. I multiply $Y_{\mathrm{He}}$ with a factor $1/2$ since the helium atom has two the Using big bang nucleosyntheis, I assume that molecules in the unvierse are in the form of either hydron or helium with the fraction of hydrogen $Y_{\mathrm{H}} = 0.75$ and the fraction of helium is $Y_{\mathrm{He}} = 0.25$.
    
    \item Hydrogn and helium are fully ionized for $z \lesssim 3$ \citep{RevModPhys.81.1405}, meaning that the ionized fraction of Hydrogen $X_{\mathrm{e, H}}(z)$ and $X_{\mathrm{e, He}}(z)$ are both equal to 1.
    
    \item The fraction of free electrons in the intergalactic medium $f_{\mathrm{IGM}}$ is constant for the range of redshifts considered in this problem{\color{red} find a citations for this}.
\end{itemize}
By dropping the redshift dependence on the ionized fraction, I get an expression of $F(z)$ that is constant in redshift for the range of redshifts considered in this thesis,
\begin{equation}
    F(z) = f_{\mathrm{IGM}}\left[Y_{\mathrm{H}}X_{\mathrm{e, H}} + \frac{1}{2}Y_{\mathrm{He}} X_{\mathrm{e, He}}\right],
\end{equation}
Writing $\bar{\rho}_\mathrm{b} = \Omega_\mathrm{b} \rho_{\mathrm{cr}}$, The final form of Eq. \ref{eq:dispersion_ne} is given by
\begin{equation} \label{eq:dispersion_final}
    \mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{LSS}(z) = \mathcal{A} \int_0^z \frac{1+z'}{E(z')} \mathrm{d}z' \, ,
\end{equation}
with $\mathcal{A} := \frac{3c\Omega_{\mathrm{b0}}H_0}{8\pi G m_{\mathrm{p}}} \chi_{\mathrm{e}} f_{\mathrm{IGM}}$, and $E(z) = H(z)/H_0$. This is the expression for the DM that rises from free electrons that follows the large scales structure, hence, I will label the DM that rises from the cosmic electron density with $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{LSS}$ hereafter. As seen from \ref{eq:dispersion_final},  $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{LSS}$ is highly sensitive to he baryon density in the universe $\Omega_\mathrm{b}$ and the Hubble constant $H_0$. 

Eq. \ref{eq:dispersion_final} expresses the mean DM at a redshift $z$. For a perturbed universe, the electron number density field is also a function of the angular direction $\mathbf{\hat{x}}$. I defined the electron number density constrast $\delta_\mathrm{e}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}, z)$ in terms of the mean electorn density $n_\mathrm{e}(z)$ and the electron density in a perturbed universe $n_\mathrm{e}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}, z)$
\begin{equation}
    \delta_\mathrm{e}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}, z) = \frac{n_\mathrm{e}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}, z) - n_\mathrm{e}(z)}{n_\mathrm{e}(z)}.
\end{equation}
The corresponding $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{LSS}$ is therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:DM_inhomogeneous}
    \mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{LSS}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}, z) = \mathcal{A} \int_0^z \frac{1+z'}{E(z')} (1 + \delta_\mathrm{e}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}, z')) \mathrm{d}z' \, .
\end{equation}
It is clear that $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{LSS}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}, z)$ is a random field since it is related to $\delta_\mathrm{e}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}, z')$ which in turn is related to the matter density contrast $\delta$. 

Using the formalism introduced in sections \ref{theory_sec:statistics} and \ref{theory_sec:halo_model}, it is possible to use the electron power spectrum $P_{\mathrm{ee}}$ to express the correlation between two measured $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{LSS}$ at a angular positions $\left(\mathbf{\hat{x}}_i, \mathbf{\hat{x}}_j\right)$ and at redshifts $(z_i, z_j)$, namely the covariance matrix. \citep{10.1093/mnras/stad1645} showed that the cosmological covariance of  $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{LSS}$ is derivd by first writing the correlator bracket 
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{cov}_{ij} = \langle\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{LSS}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}_i, z_i) \mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{LSS}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}_j, z_j)\rangle - \mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{LSS}(z_i) \mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{LSS}(z_j),
\end{equation}
Simplifying, the expression reduced to
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{cov}_{ij} = \int_0^{z_i} \mathrm{d}z_i' \, W_{\mathrm{DM}}^i(z_i') \int_0^{z_j} \mathrm{d}z_j' \, W_{\mathrm{DM}}^j(z_j') \left\langle \delta_{\mathrm{e}}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}_i, z_i') \delta_{\mathrm{e}}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}_j, z_j') \right\rangle,
\end{equation}
where I have defined the weight $W_{\mathrm{DM}}(z)$ as
\begin{equation}
    W_{\mathrm{DM}}(z) = \begin{cases} \mathcal{A} \dfrac{1+z}{E(z)} & z < z_{\mathrm{FRB}} \\ 0 & z > z_{\mathrm{FRB}} \end{cases},
\end{equation}
with $z_\mathrm{FRB} = z_i, z_j$ the redshift of an FRB. The electron correlator bracket in real space can be written in harmonic space such that
\begin{equation} \label{eq:e_fourier}
    \left\langle \delta_{\mathrm{e}}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}_i, z_i) \delta_{\mathrm{e}}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}_j, z_j) \right\rangle = \int \frac{\mathrm{d}^3k}{(2\pi)^3} \, e^{i\mathbf{k} \cdot (\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j)} P_{\mathrm{ee}}(k, z_i, z_j),
\end{equation}
where I define the power spectrum in Fourier space in similar fashion to \ref{eq:power_spectrum_tracers} as
\begin{equation}
    \langle \delta_\mathrm{e}(\mathbf{k}, z_i) \delta_\mathrm{u}^*(\mathbf{k}', z_j) \rangle = (2\pi)^3 P_{uu}(k, z_i, z_j) \delta_D(\mathbf{k} - \mathbf{k}'),
\end{equation}
and $\mathbf{x}$ is the cosmoving spatial position and related to the comoving distance as $\mathbf{x} = (\chi \mathbf{\hat{x}}, \chi)$.

To perform the integral in \ref{eq:e_fourier}, I work in spherical coordinate sin the $k$-space, and decompose the integral into the radiu $k$ and a solid angle $\Omega_k$. In addition, I expand the exponential $e^{i\mathbf{k} \cdot (\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j)}$ into plane waves (As explained in the appendix)
\begin{align}
&\left\langle \delta_{\mathrm{e}}(\hat{x}_i, z_i) \delta_{\mathrm{e}}(\hat{x}_j, z_j) \right\rangle \notag \\[8pt]
&= \frac{2}{\pi} \int k^2 \mathrm{d}k \int \mathrm{d}\Omega_k \, P_{\mathrm{ee}}(k, z_i, z_j) \notag \\[8pt]
&\quad \times \sum_{\ell, \ell'} \sum_{m, m'} \mathrm{i}^\ell (-\mathrm{i})^{\ell'} Y_{\ell m}(\hat{k}) \, Y^*_{\ell m}(\hat{x}_i) \, j_\ell(k\chi_i) \, Y^*_{\ell' m'}(\hat{k}) \, Y_{\ell' m'}(\hat{x}_j) \, j_{\ell'}(k\chi_j) \notag \\[8pt]
&= \frac{2}{\pi} \int k^2 \mathrm{d}k \, P_{\mathrm{ee}}(k, z_i, z_j) \sum_{\ell} \sum_{m} Y^*_{\ell m}(\hat{x}_i) \, j_\ell(k\chi_i) \, Y_{\ell m}(\hat{x}_j) \, j_\ell(k\chi_j) \notag \\[8pt]
&= \frac{1}{2\pi^2} \sum_{\ell} (2\ell+1) \int k^2 \mathrm{d}k \, P_{\mathrm{ee}}(k, z_i, z_j) \, j_\ell(k\chi_i) \, j_\ell(k\chi_j) \, P_\ell(\cos\theta),
\end{align}
with $Y_{\ell m}$ and $j_\ell$ being the spherical harmonic function and the the spherical Bessel function of the first kind respectively. To simplify the sum, I use the property 
\begin{equation} \label{eq:spherical_property}
    \sum_m Y_{\ell m}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}_i) Y^*_{\ell m}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}_j) = \frac{2\ell+1}{4\pi} P_\ell(\cos\theta),
\end{equation}
with $\cos\theta_{ij} =\mathbf{\hat{x}}_i . \mathbf{\hat{x}}_j $ and $P_\ell$ is the Legendre polynomial of degree $\ell$ (defined in the appendix). In addition, the power spectrum at two as a funciton of two redshifts $z_i$ and $z_j$ can be replaced by its geometric mean such that $P_{\mathrm{ee}}(k, z_i, z_j) = \sqrt{P_{\mathrm{ee}}(k, z_i) P_{\mathrm{ee}}(k, z_j)}$ \citep{PhysRevD.95.063522}. The final expression for the cosmological covariance of $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{LSS}$ is
\begin{align}\label{eq:cosmological_covariance}
\mathrm{cov}_{ij}(\cos\theta, z_i, z_j) &= \frac{1}{2\pi^2} \sum_{\ell} (2\ell+1) P_\ell(\hat{x}_i \cdot \hat{x}_j) \int k^2 \mathrm{d}k \notag \\[8pt]
&\quad \times \int_0^{z_i} \mathrm{d}z_i' \, W_{\mathrm{DM}}^i(z_i') \sqrt{P_{\mathrm{ee}}(k, z_i')} \, j_\ell(k\chi_i) \notag \\[8pt]
&\quad \times \int_0^{z_j} \mathrm{d}z_j' \, W_{\mathrm{DM}}^j(z_j') \sqrt{P_{\mathrm{ee}}(k, z_j')} \, j_\ell(k\chi_j) \notag \\[8pt]
&= \sum_{\ell} \frac{2\ell+1}{4\pi} P_\ell(\cos\theta_{ij}) C_{ij}(\ell).
\end{align}

The expression contains two integral where one integral goes over the sacle $k$ and two integrals to include the $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{LSS}$ contribution from redshift $z = 0$ up to the redshift of the FRB $z = z_i , \, z_j$. In this thesis, fast calculation of the covariance matrix is essential since I use a compression step to pass one simulation instance in the simulation based inference pipeline. Therefore, I use the Limber approximation \citep{limber1953analysis} to accelerate the calculation of the covaraince. I show a comparison between the Limber approximation and the non-Limber approximation in section \ref{meth_sec:angular_power_spectrum}. The Limber approximation assumes tha the spherical bessel function $j_\ell$ can be effectively replaced by a Dirac-Delta distribution such that
\begin{equation} \label{eq:limber_ass}
    j_\ell(k \chi) \to \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2(\ell + \frac{1}{2})}} \, \delta_D((\ell + \frac{1}{2}) - k\chi).
\end{equation}
The word \textit{effectively} is important in this context since the $j_\ell$ by itself is \textbf{not} approximated by a the expression in Eq. \ref{eq:limber_ass}, however, the two resultant integrals are equivilant in some regimes of $\ell$. Invoking, the approximation in the full expression of the covariance and changing the integration vaiable into the comoving radius $\chi$, I obtain the final expression
\begin{equation}
    C_{ij}(\ell) = \int_0^{\min(\chi_i , \chi_j )}  \, \frac{\mathrm{d}\chi}{\chi^2} \, \frac{\mathrm{d}z}{\mathrm{d}\chi} \, \frac{\mathrm{d}z}{\mathrm{d}\chi}  \,W_{\mathrm{DM}}^j(z(\chi)) \, W_{\mathrm{DM}}^i(z(\chi)) \, P_{\mathrm{ee}}\!\left(\frac{\ell + 1/2}{\chi}, z'(\chi)\right).
\end{equation}
The choice of the Limber approximation is valid when the terms multiplied by $j_\ell$ are slowly changing realtive to $j_\ell$. The calculation via the Limber approximation agrees with the brute force calcualtion for large $\ell$ i.e the regime where $j_\ell$ oscillates very quickly. This is evident by noticing that error on the Limber approximation scales with $\mathcal{O}(\ell^\frac{1}{2})$.

Eq. \ref{eq:cosmological_covariance} has another bottleneck. The sum over the Legendre polynomials is another computationally expensive step that scales as  $\mathcal{O}(\ell)$. At small enough angular separations, it is possible to approximate the spherical coordinates as flat, in what is called the flat sky approximations \citep{kaiser1992weak}. In this approximation, the relationship between the angular power spectrum is derived by taking the Fourier tansform in two-dimesions of the field instead of the spherical harmonic transform. Starting from the last line of Eq. \ref{eq:cosmological_covariance}, the property in Eq. \ref{eq:spherical_property}, and the definition of the angular power spectrum in Eq. \ref{eq:ang_power_spectrum}, the covariance can be re-written as\footnote{I omit the subscript from $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{LSS}$ hereafter, where the symbol $\mathrm{DM}$ refers to the cosmological contributiuon to the DM.}
\begin{equation} \label{eq:cov_real}
    \mathrm{cov}_{ij} = \langle \mathrm{DM}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}_i, z_i) \mathrm{DM}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}_j, z_j)\rangle,
\end{equation}
where I used 
\begin{equation}
    \langle \mathrm{DM}_{\ell m}(z_i) \mathrm{DM}_{\ell' m'}(z_j)\rangle = \delta_{\ell \ell'} \delta_{m m'}C_{ij}(\ell),
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{DM}_{\ell m}(z_i) = \sum_{\ell m}   Y_{\ell m}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}_i) \mathrm{DM}_{\ell m}(z_i).
\end{equation}
To invoke the flat sky approximation, I invoke the Fourier transform of $ \mathrm{DM}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}_i, z_i)$ in Eq. \ref{eq:cov_real} and assume that in the two-dimensional Fourier space
\begin{equation}
     \langle \mathrm{DM}(\ell, z_i) \mathrm{DM}(\ell', z_j)\rangle = \left(2\pi\right)^2 \delta^{D}(\ell - \ell') C_{ij}(\ell),
\end{equation}
I end up with the following expression 
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{cov}_{ij} = \int \mathrm{d} \boldsymbol{\ell} e^{-i\boldsymbol{\ell}. \boldsymbol{\theta}_{ij}} C_{ij}(\ell),
\end{equation}
with volume element can be written as $\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{\ell} = \ell \mathrm{d}\ell \mathrm{d}\alpha$, and the integral can be performed in the angular position of the Fourier space $\alpha$. I use the properties of the Bessel function of the first kind
\begin{equation}
    J_0(\ell \theta_{ij}) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} e^{i\ell \theta_{ij} \cos\alpha} \, \mathrm{d}\alpha,
\end{equation}
The covariance matrix in the flat sky approximation is
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{cov}_{ij} = \int  \, \frac{\ell \mathrm{d}\ell}{2\pi} \, J_0(\ell \theta_{ij}) \, C_{ij}(\ell).
\end{equation}
This result is very useful to further accelerate the calculation of the covariance matrix. Though only valid on small angular separation, this apprixomation is widely used for forecasting purposes. Simulating the dispersion to fulfill the puprpose of this thesis will be described in section \ref{meth_sec:sim_dm}. 

Now that an expression of the covariance is obtained, I focus on deriving an expression for the variance of the dispersion measure signal at a certain redshift $z$. The variance of the DM sinal is defined as
\begin{equation}
    \sigma^2\left[\mathrm{DM}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}, z)\right] = \langle \left((\mathrm{DM}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}, z) - \mathrm{DM}(z))^2\right)\rangle.
\end{equation}
The expression can be easily derived from Eq. \ref{eq:cosmological_covariance} by setting $\mathbf{\hat{x}}_i = \mathbf{\hat{x}}_j$ and $z_i = z_j$ writing $C_{ij}(\ell) = C(\ell, z)$, yielding
\begin{equation}
    \sigma^2\left[\mathrm{DM}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}, z)\right] = \sum_{\ell} \frac{2\ell+1}{4\pi} C(\ell, z),
\end{equation}
and if I use the flat sky approximation, the expression changes to
\begin{equation}
    \sigma^2\left[\mathrm{DM}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}, z)\right] = \int  \, \frac{\ell \mathrm{d}\ell}{2\pi} \,  C(\ell, z).
\end{equation}
In total, the variance of the DM field is an importnat quantity for constraining baryonic feedback. As you can see from Eq. \ref{eq:dispersion_final}, the mean DM-$z$ relation is strongly sensitive to the value of the baryonic density ratio $\Omega_\mathrm{b}$ and the Hubble constant $H_0$ and dependent on the other density ratios through the rate of expansion $E(z)$. Therefore, the dependednce on the baryonic feedback paramerters is entirely encoded in $\sigma^2\left[\mathrm{DM}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}, z)\right]$ and the electron density contrast $\delta_\mathrm{e}$. I will explain how to model the electron power spectrum using an analytical model of the baryonic feedback in section \ref{meth_sec:bfc}. Fig. \ref{theory_fig:DM-z} shows a plot of the dipersion measure redshift relation for different values of $\Omega_\mathrm{b}$ and differnet values for baryonic feedback parameters.

In reality, the total measured $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{tot}$ receives contributions from the Milkyway galaxy and the host galaxy contribution which are not included in Eq. \ref{eq:dispersion_final}. We label each of these contribution with $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{MW}$ and $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{host}$ respectively. We discuss these two contributions in section \ref{theory_sec:uncertainties}. \end{equation}

To forward model observations, we convolve the $DM-z$ represented by $P(DM|z)$ with measurement and systematic uncertainties, in addition to other observational effects. A catalog of detected FRBs is expected to have observed dispersion measure $DM_\text{tot}^i$, galactic coordinates, and redshifts for a subset of and observations $z^i$. We assume that each detected FRB was emitted at redshift $z^i$ where the difference between identified and unidentified host can be integrated in how $P(z^i)$ is modeled. Assume that we observe $N_{FRB}$ fast radio bursts signal. We express the observations as the probability of detecting an FRB $DM_\text{tot}$ given a set of estimated redshifts $\{z^i\}$, can be decomposed using the Bayes rule such that

\begin{equation}
P(DM_\text{tot}|\{z^i\}) \propto P(\{z^i\}|DM_\text{tot}) P(DM_\text{tot}).
\end{equation}
Furthermore, if we assume that each observation $z_i$ is independent of other observations we may use the Bayes rule to decompose $P(\{z^i\}|DM_\text{tot})$ such that
\begin{equation} \label{eq:step2}
    P(\{z^i\}|DM_\text{tot}) = \prod_{i}P(z^i|DM_\text{tot}).
\end{equation}
Furthermore, the individual terms in Eq. \ref{eq:step2} could be derived using the joint probability distribution of the observed and the dispersion measure contribution from the large scale structure, namely $DM_\text{tot}$ and $DM$ such that
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
    P(z^i|DM_\text{tot}) & \propto P(DM_\text{tot}|z^i) P(z^i) \\
                    & = P(z^i) \int P(DM_\text{tot}, DM|z^i)\mathrm{d}DM \\
                    & = P(z^i) \int P(DM_\text{tot}|z^i, DM) P(DM|z^i)\mathrm{d}DM.
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
The final expression that relates the probability distribution function of an observation to other modelled terms is 
\begin{equation} \label{eq:obs_prob}
\begin{split}
    P(DM_\text{tot}|\{z^i\}) \propto & \\
    & P(DM_\text{tot}) \prod_{i}P(z^i) \int P(DM_\text{tot}|z^i, DM) P(DM|z^i)\mathrm{d}DM.
\end{split}
\end{equation}

Furthermore, to model each component of the resulting expression, we start with $P(DM_\text{tot})$. There are three components that contribute additively to $DM_\text{tot}$: the large scale structure contribution $DM_{\text{LSS}}$, the Milkyway contribution $DM_{\text{MW}}$, and the host galaxy contribution $DM_{\text{host}}$, and the observed dispersion measure is given as

\begin{equation}
    DM_\text{tot} = DM_{\text{LSS}} + DM_{\text{MW}} + DM_{\text{host}}.
\end{equation}
For simplicity, we indicate to the contribution from the large-scale structure simply with $\mathrm{DM}$ which is the same in Equation Eq. \ref{eq:obs_prob}. Consider the joint probability distribution $P(DM, DM_{\text{MW}},  DM_{\text{host}})$, this can be related to $P(DM_\text{tot}) = P(DM + DM_{\text{MW}} + DM_{\text{host}})$ via the constrained integral

\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
    P(DM_\text{tot}) &= P(DM + DM_{\text{MW}} + DM_{\text{host}}) \\
    & = \int_{\mathcal{C}} P(DM, DM_{\text{MW}},  DM_{\text{host}}) \text{ d}DM \text{ d}DM_{\text{MW}}
    \text{ d}DM_{\text{tot}} \\ 
    & = \int_{\mathcal{C}} P(DM)P(DM_{\text{MW}})  P(DM_{\text{host}}) \text{ d}DM \text{ d}DM_{\text{MW}}
    \text{ d}DM_{\text{tot}},
\end{split}
\end{equation}
Where $\mathcal{C} = \{DM, DM_{\text{MW}},  DM_{\text{host}}; DM_{\text{tot}} = DM + DM_{\text{MW}} + DM_{\text{host}}\}$
Similarly, I can model $P(DM_\text{tot}|z^i, DM)$ as 

\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        P(DM_\text{tot}|z^i, DM)  &=  \int_{\mathcal{C}} P(DM|z^i, DM)P(DM_{\text{MW}}|z^i, DM)  \\ &P(DM_{\text{host}}|z^i, DM) \text{ d}DM \text{ d}DM_{\text{MW}}
    \text{ d}DM_{\text{tot}},
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
Each of the terms making up the integral could be further reduced 


\begin{align}

    P(DM_{\text{MW}}|z^i, DM) & = P(DM_{\text{MW}}) \\
    P(DM_{\text{host}}|z^i, DM) & \propto P(DM_{\text{host}}|z^i)P(DM|z^i).
\end{align}

\section{The systematic uncertainties} \label{theory_sec:uncertainties}
In this section, I describe the modelling of systematic uncertainties on the dispersion measure. 

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Milkyway contribution:} The Milkyway (MW) galaxy, just like any disc galaxy, has free electrons in the interstellar medium (ISM). As the FRB pulse pass through the interstellar medium, it gets further dispersed by the free electron in the interstellar medium, adding an additional contribution to the measured $\mathrm{DM}_{\mathrm{tot}}$. There are curently no analytical model that can accuratley describe the electron number density in the MW, however I use the NPE2001 model which is an empirical model that is calibrated on quasars data, and which we will use in this thesis \citep{ocker2024ne2001p}. Nonetheless, the model is highly uncertain near the galactic plane meaning that subtracting $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{MW}$ obtained from this model from $\mathrm{DM}_{tot}$ leads to large measurement error if the FRB source is located near the galactic plane. Fig \ref{theory_fig:MW_map} shows a map of the MW contribution to DM as predicted by the NE2001 model.

    \centering
    % \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{}
    \caption{A map of the MW contribution to the measured DM with the NPE2001 model. The model is calibrated on data from quasars. As seen from the map, $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{MW}$ is large a the galactic plane and lower towards the pole since baryons in the MW are situated in a disk-like shape.}
    \label{theory_fig:MW_map}
\end{figure}


\item \textbf{Host galaxy contribution:} FRB picks up a contribution from the galaxy that hosts the FRB source, namely the host galaxy. To model the host galaxy contribtion, N-body simulation provide the best information to date. Using the CAMELS simulation \citep{Villaescusa-Navarro_2021}, \citet{theis2024galaxy} showed that the variability of the host contribution with redshift is mainly driven by the variability of star formation with redshift while the placement of the FRB source in the host galaxy does not impact the mean of the host contribution across different redshifts \footnote{Sources that lie at the further end of the galaxy along the line of sight will pick up higher contribution as compared to a source at the near end of a galaxy, however, on average, the mean of the distribution does not change.}. Nonetheless, varying the feedback mechanism does impact the distrubtion of the host contribution. Therefore, \citet{reischke2024analytical} provided an analytic model of the DM host contribution using the baryonificaiton correction model \citep{Schneider_Kovač_etal._2025,Schneider_Teyssier_2015}. This model was able to reproduce the probability distribution of the host contribution given redshift and halo mass as found from the CAMELS simulation suit.
However, integrating this impact in a cosmological analysis will double the dimensionality of the paramter space since another copy of baryonic feedback parameters are needed to account for the host contribution beside the cosmological contribution of the dispersion measure $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{LSS}$. In addition, the analytical model reuquires simplistic assumption regarding the distribution of stars in halos.

In this work, I assume that the host contribution follows a lognormal distribution since it fits the analytical prediction from  \citet{reischke2024analytical} and the simulation results from \citet{theis2024galaxy} for a sample size of $10^4$ FRBs, which what we consider in this thesis. In general, if the lognormal distribution is parameterized by $\mu$ and $\sigma_\mathrm{LN}^2$, then the probability distribution funciton of the host contribution is given as
\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{host}}(\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{host};\mu,\sigma_{\mathrm{LN}}) = \frac{1}{\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{host}\sigma_{\mathrm{LN}}\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{(\ln \mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{host} - \mu)^2}{2\sigma_{\mathrm{LN}}^2}\right),
\end{equation}
where $\exp(\mu)$ and $\exp(2\mu + \sigma_{\mathrm{LN}}^2)\left[\exp(\sigma_{\mathrm{LN}}^2) - 1\right]$ are the median and the variance of the distribution. 

\item \textbf{Redshift uncertainty:} Follow up optical observations of FRBs allows spectroscopic estimation of the redshifts of its host galaxy. The measurement uncertainty on the redshift can induce additional scatter in the estimated cosmological covariance matrix and modify the source redshift distribution. The latter effect is especially important when cross-correlating FRBs with cosmic shear as performed by \citet{reischke2023calibrating}. A faster way to localize FRBs is to use wide field spectroscopic or photometric surveys by deriving an astrometric solution, yielding a set of possible candidates of the host with a probability for each canditate \citep{garcia2025search}. This method is, nonetheless, highly uncertain. Therefore, the redshift uncertainty is an important systematic uncertainty to mitigate in an analysis involving FRBs.

In this work, I assume that the measured that a redshift measuremnet $z_i$ is sampled from a Gaussian distribution with mean $z_i$ and standard deviation $\sigma_i = \eta z_i$, where $\eta_i$ is a number between $0$ and $1$.


\item \textbf{Selection effects:} The time resolution of radio telescope and its ability to detect a wide range of DM values is limited by the instrument. In other words, radio telescopes are only able to detect DM values up to a certain upper limit. THis effect originate from dispersive smearing in the different radio frequency channels in the telescop, leading to an artificial broadening of the signal and low signal-to-noise ratio \citep{hoffmann2025modelling}. In general, this affects the normalization of the PDF of measured DM and not the shape. Therfore, the search efforts are restricted to a $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{\max}$, depending on the instrument. Table \ref{tab:dmmax} shows some exmaples for radio surrveys and their correponding $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{\max}$. Since $\mathrm{DM}$ is an integrated value, a lower value of $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{\max}$ restricts the search to inlcude low redshifts only.

The impact of selection was studied by \citet{sharma2026quantifying}. In this work, the authors use a probabilistic model to describe the DM with all the possible selection effect. Beside the selection based on $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{\max}$, the authors consider the redshift source distribution by modeling with 3 free parameters and fitting this model to data along with the cosmological and baryonic feedback parameters. Furthermore, this work concludes that selection effects lead to $3\sigma$ bias in the inferred paramters for $10^4$ FRBs.

In this thesis, I fix the redshift source distribution to coincide with that expected from the Square Kilometer Array Observatory (SKAO) \citep{braun2015advancing}, and impose a smooth cut on the measured $DM$ by multiplying the PDF $P(\mathrm{DM}^i ; \{z_\mathrm{FRB}^i\})$ with a sigmoid kernel \citep{shannon2018dispersion} parameterized by $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{\max}$ and the smoothness of the cut $\epsilon$

\begin{equation}
    \sigma (\mathrm{DM}; \mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{max}, \epsilon) = \frac{1}{1 + \exp\left(\frac{\mathrm{DM} - \mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{max}}{\epsilon}\right)}
\end{equation}
Therefore, for a simulated catalog, each value of $DM$, is weighed with $\sigma (\mathrm{DM}; \mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{max}, \epsilon)$. 

\item \textbf{Unlocalised hosts:} So far, the number of detected FRBs is ~3000\footnote{\href{https://www.chime-frb.ca/catalog2}{CHIME/FRB Catalog 2.}

}, and only 100 of them has identified host \citep{Reischke:2025srr} i.e only the redshifts of 100 FRBs is known. This poses as an obstacle to use all FRBs for cosmological inference since redshifts are important to calculate data covariance and constrain the DM-$z$ relationship. Since follow-up optical observation to identify the host galacxy is both time and resource consuming, it is important to realised the best strategy to distribute these resources. 
In this thesis, I explore whether constraining the DM-$z$ relation is possible if only a subset of the observed FRBs has an identified host. I explore the following cases
\begin{itemize}
    \item A random subset of FRBs has no identified hosts.
    \item A subset of high redshfit FRBs has no identified hosts. 
    \item A subset of low redshfit FRBs has no identified hosts.
\end{itemize}
Retrospectively, FRBs with high redshift has higher signal-to-noise since they pick higher contribution from the electorn density along their path to Earth, however, the measurement uncertainty of their redshift is high. Meanwhile, FRBs with low redshifts has lower signal-noise-ratio since the path they travel is less as compared to high redshift sources, however, their measured redshfits have lower uncertainties. The results will show whether observing high- or low- redshift sources will be the best stretegy to follow for follow-up optical observations. 
\end{itemize}
