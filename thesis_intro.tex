\chapter{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%==============================================================================
The questions on how did the universe came to be, why is the universe as such, and what is the universe heading towards falls upon cosmology to answer. Cosmology is the study of the universe on large scales in an attempt to answer the latter questions {\color{green} cite maybe Peebles}. This pursuit started as a data-starved research {\color{green} cite} and transformed into a data-rich endevour as a result of a platitutde of imaging surveys such as the LSST Vera Rubin observatory, Euclid, and Nancy Roman {\color{green} cite}. The qualitative questions, with which this paragraph started,< transcribe in the statistical sense to be what is the cosmological model that best describes the universe on throughout length scales and energy scales. 

The first attempt to describe the universe as a whole came through the lens of the general theory of relativity where Robertson and Walker  {\color{green} cite} built on the work of Friedmann and Lemaître to describe geometry of the universe through what is now known as the Friedmann-Lemaître-Robertson-Walker (FLRW) metric. We discuss this metric further in \ref{theory_sec:FLRW}. This metric was largely motivated by the observations of Edwin Hubble who saw that galaxy further away from the Earth are moving faster than galaxies closer to Earth {\color{green} cite}. Figure shows the plot from Hubble's work in 1929. 

Further observations of the magnitude of distant galaxies have shown that the universe is expanding {\color{green} cite}. The general theory of relativity attributes this expansion to the energy density content of the universe. Therefore, the question of what is driving the expansion of the universe and what are the componenets that contribute to the energy budget of the universe represent a crucial question in cosmology for which multiple cosmological models have been suggested \citep{di2025cosmoverse}. In fact, the cosmological model has been motivated by both cosmological and astrophysical observation. Some of these observation are the measurement of galaxy rotation curves by Vera Rubin {\color{green} cite} that hinted at an additional matter component that does not interact with light and the discovery of the cosmic microwave background (CMB) radiation that hinted at hot and homogeneous early universe.{\color{green} cite}. Furthermore,early observaiton of clusters of galaxies have shown that the spatial distribution of galaxy deviates from randomness. This was shown by Peebles {\color{green} cite}. By calculating the two point correlation function, there was clearly a signal in the way that galaxies are distributed which points out to an underlying mechanism in the formation of these galaxies \citep{Totsuji_Kihara_1969}. The development of obsrevational techniques and instrumentation allowed the launch of the Center for Astrophysics Redshift Survey \citep{Davis_Huchra_Latham_Tonry_1982} which showed that galaxy positions are correlated on large scales and that there is a structure in the universe \citep{Davis_Peebles_1983}.

In the early 1990s, cosmology experienced a paradigm shift with the launch of the Cosmic Background Explorer (COBE) sattelite \citep{Boggess_Mather_etal._1992}. The COBE mission provided the first detection of primordial CMB anisotropies and further confirmed that the universe is flat \citep{Smoot_1999}. In the context of the FLRW matric, the flatness of the universe requires a total sum of the relative density parameters to be 1 (within error bounds). Therefore, in addition to the matter component (dark and baryons), there should be a third component that can account for the missing energy density. Using supernova data from the Hubble Space Telescope \citep{Bahcall_Spitzer_1982}, \citet{Riess_etal._1998} found a strong evidence for an expanding universe universe with negative acceleration and supported the findings of the COBE mission for the existance of additional density component different from matter which is driving the expansion of the universe. Hereafter, the term 'dark energy was coined to refer to this component \citep{Turner_2001}. Wiht this, the $\Lambda$CDM model was born, and the efforts to obtain a precise estimates of the cosmological parameters intensified \citep{Bennett_etal._2003, Pope_2004}.

While early experiements relied on CMB, galaxy number counts, or supernova to infer cosmological parameters, gravitational lensing was in development as a tool to constrain the cosmological parameters. Gravitational lensing is the distortions of light geodesics by gravity when light travels from a distant source travels through the inhomogeneous universe towards an observer on the Earth \citep{Schneider_Ehlers_Falco}. On the level, of a galaxy, gravitional lensing effects manifest themselves as a distortion of the shape of the galaxy and a change in the angular position with respect to a particular observer. \citet{Kaiser_Squires_1993} showed that these distortions could be used to obtain a map of the integrated mass density along the line of sight. The use of galaxy shape distortions for cosmology was a direct result of the Kairser-Squire inversion formula where \citet{seitz1996steps} realised that the expectation value of galaxy shape is an unbiased estimate of the shear in the weak field limit. In principle, it is possible to relate the power spectrum of galaxy shapes with the matter power spectrum, offering a concrete method of probing cosmology by studying the correlations between galaxy shapes \citep{Bartelmann_Schneider_2001}. This new probe of the large scale structure is called cosmic shear and was used by \citet{Maoli_VanWaerbeke_2001} to obtain constraints on the cosmological parameters. These constraints were not as tight as the ones obtained by COBE, however, it paved the road towards using cosmic shear to constrain the cosmological model. The advantage of cosmic shear as an integrated quantitity along the line of sight mean that it is sensitive to structure growth in the late universe. Therefore, it represent a very good tool to constrain the equation of state for dark energy \citep{Hu_Tegmark_1999, Jain_Taylor_2003}.

After that, multiple galaxy surveys used cosmic shear for cosmilogical parameter inference, and numerous statistical tools were developped to deal with the increasing data size \citep{Schneider_VanWaerbeke_Jain_Kruse_1998, Schneider_Eifler_Krause_2010}. Likewise, with increasing data size, the noise level in a cosmological signal increases and the need to mititgate the impact of the systematic and measreument uncertainties is imperative. In this work, we focus on tackling on of the most crucial systematics, namely baryonic feedback on the large scale structure.

Baryonic feedback are the set of baryonic physics processes that impact the distribution of the matter \citep{Rudd_Zentner_Kravtsov_2008}. These processes include, but not limited to, supernova feedback, active galactic nuclei feedback, and star formation. By analying hydrodynamical simulations,  \citet{van2011effects} realised that baryonic feedback affect the matter distribution in the universe at different scales, meaning that their impact should be modelled and included in a theorey of structure formation. However, the theoretical formalism of structure growth, and therefore the matter power spectrum assumes that structure growth is only driven by gravitional effects, ignoring baryonic feedback \citep{Dodelson_Schmidt_2026}. Modelling baryonic feedback analytically is a cumbersome task, especially that the physics of galaxy formation and evolution is highly uncertain where different suits of hydrodunamical simulations have a different feedback models, and each one of these model yields a totally different desciption of the matter power spectrum supression \citep{van2011effects}. The mismodelling of the matter power spectrum tunnels down to the modelled cosmic shear signal and yields biased parameter estimates in a comsmological analysis.

On a conquest for precision cosmology, multiple statistical and modelling methods attempted to mititgate the impact of baryonic feedback on the large scale structure. For example, \citet{krause2021dark} imposed scle cuts on measured shear correlation funciton and only considered larger scales where baryonic feedback is minimal. However, the cosmic shear signal derives most of its sensitivity from non-linear scale i.e. small scales \citep{Piccirilli_Zennaro_García-García_Alonso_2025}. Therefore, imposing a scale cut lead to loss of information. Other statistical techniques involve the use of principle component analysis to compress the parameter space of baryonic feedback parameters from hydrodynamical simulations and marginlising over these parameters in a cosmological analysis \citep{Eifler_Krause_Dodelson_Zentner_Hearin_Gnedin_2015}. Another statistical method involves introducing an additive term to the covariance matrix called the theoretical error covariance \citep{Moreira_2021, Maraio_2025a} which descibe the variation of different baryonic feedback scenarios among all available hydrodynamical simualtions. Analyses involving all the afformentioned results yields unbiased estimates of the cosmological parameters, however, it comes at a cost of introducing additional scatter due to the existing nuisanc parameters. Finally, \citet{Schneider_Teyssier_2015} introduced the baryonification correction model, a model that parametrizes the displancement of dark matter particles in the existance of baryonic feedback. This model and its developments \citep{Schneider_Kovač_etal._2025} were calibrated on hydrodynamical simulations and were able to emulate baryonic feedback in other hydrodynamical simulations that they were not calibrated on. Having this model, it is possible to obtain a prediciton for any probe of baryon in the universe. In particular, this model was used to constrain baryonic feedback parameters with X-ray and the kinematic Sunyaev-Zel'dovich effect \citep{Kovač_2025} since it is possible to obtain a prediction of gas power spectra form which a simulated signal of the correponding baryon tracer could be obtained. Though these probes are promising, they rely on some assumptions with respect the structure of the galaxy clusters such as having hydrodynamic equilibrium and require many convolution from a prediciotn of the gas power spectrum up to a predicted signal. Luckily, fast radio bursts provide a direct probe of the electron density in the universe through the information that their signal encodes. 

Fast radio bursts (FRBs) are pulses of electromagnetic waves of extragalactic origin. First discovered in 2007 \citep{Lorimer_Bailes_McLaughlin_Narkevic_Crawford_2007}, the signal of FRBs gets broadened while travelling through the universe, meaning that that the wave packets of higher frquency will arrive later that wave packets of higher frquency. This difference in arrival time is directly proportional to the integrated electron density along the line of sight where the dispersion measure signal could be easily simulated with minimal assumptions about the relationship of the dispersion measure and the electron number density in the universe. The grand benefit of the dispersion measure signal lies in its constraining power of baryonic feedback parameters since it is a drirect tracer of the baryon distributino in the universe. Even though the source of these bursts is unknown \citep{Petroff_2019}, it is still possible to exploit the dispersion measure signal in a cosmological context, given that FRBs are hosted within galaxies whose redshift can be identified. 

Simulated likelihood, analyses showed that the cross-correlation of FRBs with other probes of structure yields better constraints on the some cosmological parameters. In particular, Calibrating baryonic feedback with FRBs and weak lesning led to one order of magnitude better constrains on the baryonic feedback and 50\% improvements on constraints of the sum of neutrino masses $ \sum m_\nu$ and the baryonic fraction in the unvierse $\Omega_b$ \citep{reischke2023calibrating}. Furthermore, combining FRBs with galaxy number counts in a 3x2-point analysis provided better constraints on baryonic feedback parameters as compared to galaxy number counts \citep{Sharma_2026}. In practice, we can aim to use FRBs alone as a probe to impose tigh constraints on the baryonic feedback parameters and use these constraints as priors in a cosmological analysis involving probes of structure, allowing the information content of LSS data to constrain the cosmological parameters and delegating the task of constraining baryonic feedback to FRBs.

The promising potential of FRBs in constraining baryonic feedback could be hindered by multiple modelling and measurement uncertainties. First, FRBs are observed using radio telescopes with only limited follow-up optical observations that are necessairy to calculate the redshifts of galaxies and by turn quantify the associated mean DM at that redshift. Second, measreuments of these redshifts are highly uncertain especially for sources at high redshifts. Third, the measured disperison measure signal receive three main contribution: a contribution from the Milkyway, a contribution from the host galaxy, and a contribution from the circugalactic medium which is the quantity of comsological interest \citep{Reischke:2025srr}. The models for both the Milkyway and the host galaxy contribution are both uncertain, leading to biases in the parameters constraints if not dealth with correctly. Fourth, the theoretical formalism of fast radio bursts assumes that the radio signal travels in a homogeneous universe, ignoring any gravitational lensing effect due to large scale structure. There are contradicting resutls on impact of gravitaional lensing; \citet{Alonso_2021} showed that first order contribution from general relativitstic effects to the DM angular power spectrum are subdominant compared to the contribution from the electron column density, meanwhile, \citet{Takahashi_2024} showed that the second order contribution due to lensing could lead to 1\% diviation in the luminosity distance, resulting underestimation of the the luminosity distance. 

Therefore, the purpose of this thesis is to model, forecast, and explore mitigation effects of systematic uncertainties. With upcoming radio survey, a plathora of data will be available that an accurate modelling of the dispersion measure signal is crucial to extract meanigful information from the signal \citep{LukeHoffmann_James_Glowacki_Prochaska_Gordon_Deller_Shannon_Ryder_2024}. I scale up previoud work by \citet{Konar_Reischke_Hagstotz_Nicola_Hildebrandt_2025a} by using simulation-based inference (SBI), hereafter to infer the impact of systematic uncertainties on the baryonic parameters constraints using fast radio bursts only. Using SBI in this context has multiple benefits. First, it facilitates integrating different uncertainty effects on the level the dispersion measure signal either on the level of the dispersion measure or the level of the redshift. Second, it retires the necessity to approximate the likelihood of the data given the parameters unlike traditional Baysian analyses. Results from ray tracing in hydrodynamical simualtions \citep{Konietzka:2025kdr} suggests that a Gaussian or lognormal approximation to the probability distribution function breaks down for large number of FRBs, and analytic models of the dispersino measure is needed to simulate teh PDF of FRBs. \citep{torkamani2026baryonification}. More details on SBI and the method used in this thesis could be found in \ref{meth:sbi}
