Future work should focus on scaling up to larger sample size since the impact of systematic uncertainties changes with sample size.

To model the dispersion measure signal, I use the Generator for Large-Scale Structure (GLASS) \citep{tessore2023glass} package. I describe this package in further details in section \ref{meth_sec:glass}. Briefly, the GLASS package generates lognormal maps of a cosmological field starting from a given angular power spectra. On contrary to the commonly used \texttt{healpy} package, it assumes that the cosmological field follows a lognormal distribution. Though this is not the case in for a real cosmological field, the GLASS package preserves the two-point statistics of the cosmologocal field, which is enough for the sample size I am considering in this thesis. Higher order statistics such as the bispectrum (3-point) and the trispectrum (4-point) requires a larger sample size for detection. Using the electron power spectrum, I project the spectra on the unit sphere to obtain an expectation of the angular power specta in bins of redshifts. After that, I feed the resultatnt power spectra to GLASS in order to obtain maps of electron density in bins of redshift. THe next step constitute in sampling randomly on the sky and according to the source redshift distribution in redshift then sum the contribution from different shells. \section{Modelling the electron power spectrum} \label{meth_sec:pee}
To model the electron power spectrum, I use the halo model (introduceed in section \ref{theory_sec:halo_model}), where I obtain halo profiles using the Baryonification Model (BCM) \citep{Schneider_Teyssier_2015,Schneider_Kovač_etal._2025}.


\subsection{The baryonification model} \label{meth_sec:bfc}
This section is based on the review of the baryonificaiton correction model presented in \citet{torkamani2026baryonification}.

The purpose of the baryonificaiton model (BFC) is modify the profiles of dark matter halos from N-body gravitational simulation to that which involves baryonic feedback. The change is expressed as a shift in the position of dark matter particles which is parameterized by a set of baryonic feedback parameters.The displacement for dark matter (dm) and baryonic matter (bar) is performed along the radius of a dark matter halo. In practice, the dm and the bar profiles are modified differently when baryonic feedback is taken into consideration. In this thesis, I only focus on the case of modified bar profile. In a a scenario without baryonic feedback, the baryons  follow the distribution of dark matter. The baryon halo profile $\rho_{\mathrm{bar}, i}$ can be written in terms of the underlying dark matter profile  $\rho_{\mathrm{NFW}}$\footnote{$\rho_{\mathrm{NFW}}$ is an analytical expression of dark matter halo density profiles, predicted empirically by fitting the expression to N-body gravitional simulation \citep{Navarro_1997}} on small scales in addition to a large scale contribution from the matter outside of the collapsed halo region which I label as $\rho_{\mathrm{2h}}$\footnote{This contribution is labelled similary to the 2-halo term of the power spectrum since it dominates the contribution on large scales similar to the 2-halo term of the power spectrum}. In total, the final expression reads
\begin{equation}
    \rho_{\mathrm{bar,i}}(r) = f_{\mathrm{bar}} \left[ \rho_{\mathrm{NFW}}(r) + \rho_{\mathrm{2h}}(r) \right],
\end{equation}
with $f_{\mathrm{bar}}  = \Omega_\mathrm{b}/\Omega_\mathrm{c}$ $\rho_{\mathrm{2h}}$ given in term the linear order 2-point correlation function $\xi_\mathrm{lin}$
\begin{equation}
    \rho_{\mathrm{2h}}(r) = \left[ 1 + b(\nu)\xi_{\mathrm{lin}}(r) \right] \Omega_{\mathrm{b}} \rho_{\mathrm{crit}} \, ,
\end{equation}
with $b(\nu)$ the halo bias taken from \citet{tinker2010large}. $\nu$ in this context refer to the ratio between the critical density contrast for spherical collapse at linear order $\delta_\mathrm{c, lin}$ and the standard deviation of halo mass $\sigma(M)$. 

Since baryonic feedback affects the distribution of matter on small sacles, the modified baryon profile  the component $\rho_{\mathrm{NFW}}$\footnote{$\rho_{NFW}$} should be modified. Empirically, this mean that on small scales, the contribution to the baryon profile comes primarily from gas and stars within a dark matter halo. The modified component $\rho_{\mathrm{bar,f}}$ becomes
\begin{equation}
    \rho_{\mathrm{bar,f}}(r) = \rho_{\mathrm{gas}}(r) + \rho_{\mathrm{star}}(r) + f_{\mathrm{bar}}\rho_{\mathrm{2h}}(r).
\end{equation}
Each of the densities in the previous equation is parametrized with a set of cosmological and baryonic feedback parameters. For any species $\mathrm{u}$, the cumilitive mass up to a radius $r$ is given By
\begin{equation}
    M_{\mathrm{u}}(R) = 4\pi \int_0^R r^2 \rho_{\mathrm{u}} \, \mathrm{d}r = f_{\mathrm{u}} Y_{\mathrm{u}}(R),
\end{equation}
where $f_{\mathrm{u}}$ is the abundance of a component (relative to the total matter) and $Y_{\mathrm{u}}$ is normalized such that $Y_{\mathrm{u}}(\inf) = M_\mathrm{tot}$
To qunatify the deviation $d(r)$ in a particle's position between its initial (gavitational effects only) position $r_\mathrm{bar, i}$ to its final (gravitional effects and baryonic feedback) position $r_\mathrm{bar, f}$, the radius $r_\mathrm{bar}$ as a function of the cumilitive mass $M_{\mathrm{bar}}$ is obtained
\begin{equation}
    r_{\mathrm{bar}} = \mathrm{inv}\left[ M_{\mathrm{bar}}(r) \right],
\end{equation}
and the resultant deviation is written as
\begin{equation}
    d(r) = r_{\mathrm{bar,f}} - r_{\mathrm{bar,i}}.
\end{equation}
The mapping from $r_{\mathrm{bar,i}}$ to $r_{\mathrm{bar,f}}$ is parameterized with the baryonic feedback paramters shown in Table \ref{tab:bfc_prarams}. In addition, Fig. \ref{meth_fig:bfc} shows how the BFC model changes the profiles of dark matter halos.

\subsection{The Core Cosmology Library (CCL)}
The Core Cosmology Library (CCL) \citep{chisari2019core} is a package that enables fast and efficient calculations of basic cosmological observables with high accuracy. CCL implements a Boltzmann solver in order to obtain a prediction of the linear power spectrum on a grid of redshifts $z$ and scale $k$. It also provides a bicubic interpolator for accurate calculation of the power spectrum, starting from the initial grid points. In this thesis, I sample 30 grid points in $z$ and 150 points in $k$. These are enough grid points to ensure sub-percent accuracy as compared larger numbe of grid points. However, since the $\log P (k, z)$ is linearly related to  $\log k$ for both large and small values of $k$, it is possible to extrapolate the power spectrum in logarithmic space beyond the ranges specified in Table \ref{tab:sim_spec}. In addition, to obtain the non-linear power spectrum, CCL implements the halo model where the 1-halo and the 2-halo terms are calculated using the halo profiles obtained from the BFC model. The halo mass function and the halo bias are taken from \citet{tinker2008toward} and \citet{tinker2010large}, respectively. I consider only a linear bias model where the term $\beta$ from Eq. \ref{eq:halo_spectrum} is set to $0$. I show a plot of th epower spectrum for different redshfits in Fig. \ref{meth_fig:power_spectrum}.

\subsection{The electron angular power spectrum} \label{meth_sec:angular_power_spectrum}
To obtain the electron angular power spectra in bins of redshifts, I define the electron density contrast $\delta_\mathrm{e}^i$ in a redshift bin $i$ as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:project_delta_e}
    \delta_\mathrm{e}^i(\mathbf{\hat{x}}) = \int W^i(z) \, \delta_\mathrm{e}(z, \mathbf{\hat{x}}) \, \mathrm{d}z,
\end{equation} 
with $W^i(z)$ is the normalized weight function of the redshift bin $i$. The general form of the weight funciton is a top-hat function multiplied by a weight factor
\begin{equation}
    W_i(z) = \begin{cases} f(z) & \text{if } z^{i}_\mathrm{min} \leq z < z^i_\mathrm{max}, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise,} \end{cases},
\end{equation}
where $z^{i}_\mathrm{min}$ and $z^{i}_\mathrm{max}$ are the minimum and the maximmum redshift of a shell $i$ and the form of the function $f(z)$ is chosen arbitrarly such as to account for the expansion of the Universe. I discuss the choice of $f$ in section \ref{meth_sec:glass}. The electron angular power spectrum $C_{\delta_e \delta_e}^{(ij)}(\ell)$ between two redshift bins $i$ and $j$ is given by 
\begin{align}
    C_{\delta_e \delta_e}^{(ij)}(\ell) &= \frac{2}{\pi} \int \mathrm{d}\chi \frac{\mathrm{d}z}{\mathrm{d}\chi}\, \frac{\mathrm{d}z}{\mathrm{d}\chi} \, W^{(i)}(z(\chi)) \int \mathrm{d}\chi' \frac{\mathrm{d}z}{\mathrm{d}\chi'}\, W^{(j)}(z(\chi')) \notag \\
    &\quad \int \mathrm{d}k \, k^2 \, P_\mathrm{e, nl}(k, z(\chi), z(\chi')) \, j_\ell(k\chi) \, j_\ell(k\chi'),
\end{align}
where this derivation follows similarly to the derivation of the covariance matrix in section \ref{theory_sec:FRBs}. Using the Limber approximation, the expression is simplified into
\begin{equation}
    C_{\delta_e \delta_e}^{(ij)}(\ell) = \int \mathrm{d}\chi \, \frac{\mathrm{d}z}{\mathrm{d}\chi} \,\frac{\mathrm{d}z}{\mathrm{d}\chi} \, \frac{W^{(i)}(z(\chi)) W^{(j)}(z(\chi))}{\chi^2} P_\mathrm{e, nl}\left(k = \frac{\ell + 1/2}{\chi}, z(\chi)\right).
\end{equation}
In this theis, I use th limber approximation to obtain the angular power psectrum since it is computationally effecient as compared to the non-Limber expression. At large values of $\ell$, the non-Limber expression converges to the Limber expression, as can be seen in Fig. \ref{meth_fig:cls}. $z_\mathrm{eff}$ refers ti the effective redshift of the bin which is given by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:z_eff}
    z_\mathrm{eff} = \int z W^{(i)}(z) \, \mathrm{d}z
\end{equation}




The choice of 29 redshift bin is validated by looking at the correlation betwen two neighboring shells. I define the ratio
\begin{equation}
    R = \frac{C_{\delta_e \delta_e}^{(i, i+1)}(\ell)}{\sqrt{C_{\delta_e \delta_e}^{(i, i)}(\ell) C_{\delta_e \delta_e}^{(i+1, i+1)}(\ell)}},
\end{equation}
which is a measure of the correlation between two neighboring shells. I find that for 29 redshift bins, the ratio $R$ is less than $0.1$ for all values of $\ell$, which means that the correlation between neighboring shells is small enough to be ignored in this work. I show the comparison in Fig. \ref{meth_fig:cls_shells_compared}. Since I perform the analysis on mock catalogs, I can ignore the correlation between neighboring shells which is the default when using the Limber approximation. For applicaiton on real data, the correlation between neighboring shells should be taken into consideration by using the non-Limber expression for the angular power spectrum. I show a comparison between the Limber and the non-Limber expressions in Fig. \ref{meth_fig:cls}.

\section{Simulating the dispersion measure signal} \label{meth_sec:sim_dm}
Table \ref{tab:cosmo_params} and \ref{tab:bfc_prarams} contain the fiducial values of the cosmological and baryonic feedback parameters, which are used to obtain an expectaiton of the power spectrum. In addition, Table \ref{tab:sim_spec} contains the specifications of the simulation such as the number of redshift bins and the maximum multipole. In particular the choice of maximum mutlipole $\ell_\mathrm{max} = 4100$ is motivated by the fact that this scale corresponds to a length scale of $k \approx 10 \, h/\mathrm{Mpc}$ at a redshift of $z = 1$ which is the scale where baryonic feedback modifies the power spectrum \citep{Maraio_Hall_Taylor_2025}.

\subsection{Generating electron density maps}\label{meth_sec:glass}
To generate electron density maps, I use The Generator for Large Scale Structure (GLASS) \citep{tessore2023glass} which is a package that generates lognormal maps of a cosmological field starting from a given angular power spectra. The GLASS package is used in this thesis to generate maps of electron density in bins of redshift starting from the electron angular power spectrum obtained in section \ref{meth_sec:angular_power_spectrum}. The GLASS package assumes that the cosmological field follows a lognormal distribution, which is not the case for a real cosmological field. However, the GLASS package preserves the two-point statistics of the cosmological field. 

For an arbitrary cosmological field $u$, the GLASS package assumes that the fiels follows a lognormal distribution with an underlying Gaussian field $g$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lognorma_gaussian}
    u = \lambda \left(e^g + 1\right)\,  \equiv g = \ln\left( \frac{u - 1}{\lambda} \right),
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ is a parameter that can be chosen by noticing that the lower bound of a Gaussian field that represent density perturbation is $-1$, making $\lambda = 1$. The reason behind linking the field $f$ to a field $g$ is that sampling from a Gaussian distribution is computationally efficient as compared to a lognormal distribution. 


After feeding the electron power spectrum to the GLASS package, it converts the power spectra to 2-point correlations functions $C(\theta)$ of the field $f$. Then, it uses the relation between $f$ and $u$ to obtain the 2-point correlation function $G(\theta)$ of the underlying Gaussian field $g$. The 2-point correlation function of the Gaussian field $g$ is given by
\begin{equation}
    G(\theta) = \ln\left( \frac{C(\theta)}{\lambda^2} + 1 \right).
\end{equation}
After that, $G(\theta)$ is converted to the angular electron power spectrum $G_\ell$ of the underlying Gaussian field $g$. Finally, the GLASS package samples from the Gaussian distribition with a pwoer spectrum $G_\ell$ to obtain a map of the underlying Gaussian field $g$, which is then converted to a map of the electron density field $u$ using Eq. \ref{eq:lognorma_gaussian}. The conversion between the power spectrum and the 2-point correaltion function is not trivial since it involves an infinite sum over the multipoles $\ell$ similar to \ref{eq:cosmological_covariance}, however, the GLASS package implements a solver to perform this conversion efficiently. I refer the reader to section 3.3 of \citet{tessore2023glass} for more details about the implementation of the GLASS package.

To use this package, I specify a weight function $W$ as desscribed in section \ref{meth_sec:angular_power_spectrum}. The choice of the weight function is motivated by the fact that the DM signal is a projection along the line of sight which mean that a comoving volume element at a redshift $z$ contributes to the DM signal with a weight that is proportional to the comoving volume element at that redshift. Therefore, I choose a weight function of the form
\begin{equation}
    W^{(i)}(z) = \begin{cases} A^{(i)} \chi^2(z)/E(z) & \text{if } z_{i-1} \leq z < z_i, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise,} \end{cases}.
\end{equation}
with $A^{(i)}$ being a normalization constant calculated for each bin $i$ such that 
\begin{equation}
    \int W^{(i)}(z) \, \mathrm{d}z = 1.
\end{equation}

Dividing in comoving distance rather than redshift ensures that the shells are of equal thickness in comoving space, which is more physically motivated since the DM signal is a projection along the line of sight. To sum the contritbuion from each shell, I discretize Eq. \ref{eq:DM_inhomogeneous} by splitting the mean DM contribution from the perturbed contribution such that
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{DM}(\mathbf{\hat{x}}, z) = \langle\mathrm{DM}\rangle(z) + \mathcal{D}(\hat{\mathbf{x}}, z),
\end{equation}
with $\langle\mathrm{DM}\rangle(z)$ given by Eq. \ref{eq:dispersion_final}, which can be calculated numerically, and $\mathcal{D}(\hat{\mathbf{x}}, z)$ given by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dm_perturbation}
\mathcal{D}(\hat{x}, z) = \mathcal{A} \int_0^z \frac{1+z'}{E(z')} \delta_{\mathrm{e}}(\hat{x}, z') \mathrm{d}z' \, .
\end{equation}
The relationship between $\delta_{\mathrm{e}}(\hat{x}, z)$ and $\delta_{\mathrm{e}}(\hat{x})$ is given by Eq. \ref{eq:project_delta_e}. Therefore, the contribution from a GLASS shell $i$ with an effective redshift $\bar{z}_i$ in a direction $\hat{x}$, namely $\delta_{\mathrm{e}}^{(i)}(\hat{x})$, is given in terms of the electron density contrast $\delta_{\mathrm{e}}(\hat{x}, z)$ as
\begin{equation} \label{eq:delta_e_discrete}
\delta_{\mathrm{e}}^{(i)}(\hat{x}) \approx W^{(i)}(\bar{z}_i) \delta_{\mathrm{e}}(\hat{x}, \bar{z}_i) (z_{i + 1} - z_{i}).
\end{equation}
Therefore, by discretizing Eq, \ref{eq:dm_perturbation} as 
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{D}(\hat{x}, z_\mathrm{FRB}) = \mathcal{A} \displaystyle\sum_{i = 1}^{n_\mathrm{shells}} \frac{1 + \bar{z}_i}{E(\bar{z}_i)} \bar{\delta}_\mathrm{e} (\hat{x}, \bar{z}_i) \, w_\mathrm{shell}^i(z_\mathrm{FRB}),
\end{equation}
where $w_\mathrm{shell}^i$ represent the weight a shell's contribution depending on the value of $z_\mathrm{FRB}$ as given by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cal_D_descrete}
    w_\mathrm{shell}^i(z_\mathrm{FRB}) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{, if } z_\mathrm{FRB} \geq z_\mathrm{max}^i , \\ z_\mathrm{FRB} - z_\mathrm{min}^i & \text{, if } z_\mathrm{min}^i \leq z_\mathrm{FRB} \geq z_\mathrm{max}^i\\ 0 & \text{, if } z_\mathrm{FRB} \leq z_\mathrm{min}^i \end{cases}
\end{equation}

where I redefine $w_\mathrm{shell}^i(z_\mathrm{FRB})$ as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cal_D_descrete_final}
    w_\mathrm{shell}^i(z_\mathrm{FRB}) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{, if } z_\mathrm{FRB} \geq z_\mathrm{max}^i , \\ \frac{z_\mathrm{FRB} - z_\mathrm{min}^i}{z_\mathrm{max}^i - z_\mathrm{min}^i} & \text{, if } z_\mathrm{min}^i \leq z_\mathrm{FRB} \geq z_\mathrm{max}^i\\ 0 & \text{, if } z_\mathrm{FRB} \leq z_\mathrm{min}^i \end{cases}
\end{equation}

In total, I use the above formalism in summing hte contribution from different redshift shells which will evetually lead to the large-scale structure contribution $\mathrm{DM}_i$ of an FRB at redshift $z_\mathrm{FRB}^{i}$ and sky position $(g_l^i, g_b^i)$, yielding a catalog of FRBs.

In addition to these parameters, I consider a simple model for the evolution of the host contribution with redshfit such that
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{host}(z) = \frac{\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{host}}{1 + z}
\end{equation}
with $z$ corresponding to the redshfit of the FRB. Assuming that the correlation between the $\mathrm{DM}$ and $\mathrm{DM}_\mathrm{host}$ is negligible, the covariance matrix receives an additional contribution 
\begin{equation}
    \langle \mathrm{DM}_{\mathrm{host}, i} \mathrm{DM}_{\mathrm{host}, j}\rangle =  \frac{\sigma^2_\mathrm{host}}{(1 + z_i)(1+ z_j)}
\end{equation}
with $\sigma^2_\mathrm{host}$ is the variance of the host contribution as defined in section \ref{theory_sec:uncertainties}.



\subsection{Redshift uncertainty} \label{meth_sec:z_uncertainty}
I simulate the imapct of redshift uncertainty by assuming that a redshift measuremnet $z_i$ is sampled from a Gaussian distribution with mean $z_i$ and standard deviation $\sigma_i = \eta z_i$, where $\eta_i$ is a number between $0$ and $1$. To propagate the error on the cosmological covariance, consider that $(z_i, z_j)  \sim \mathcal{N}((\hat{z}_i, \hat{z}_j) , \Sigma)$, with $\mathcal{N}$ is the normal distribution and
\begin{equation}
    \Sigma = \begin{bmatrix}
    \sigma_i^2 & 0 \\
    0 & \sigma_j^2 
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
To propagate the error inthe covariance, I calcualte the expectation value $\langle \hat{C}_{ij} \rangle$, given as
\begin{equation}
    \langle \hat{C}_{ij} \rangle = \int \mathcal{N}((\hat{z}_i, \hat{z}_j) , \Sigma) \, \, C_{ij} \, \,dz_i \, \, dz_j
\end{equation}
With Taylor's expansion around $(\hat{z}_i, \hat{z}_j)$ and setting $\Delta_{ij} = (z_i, z_j) - (\hat{z}_i, \hat{z}_j)$, the covariance matrix can be written
\begin{equation}
    \langle \hat{C}_{ij} \rangle = \int \mathcal{N} \, \, \left(\hat{C}_{ij} + \nabla C_{ij}|_{(\hat{z}_i, \hat{z}_j)} \Delta_{ij} + \frac{1}{2} \Delta_{ij}^{T} \nabla^2 C_{ij}|_{(\hat{z}_i, \hat{z}_j)}\Delta_{ij} + .....\right)\, \,dz_i \, \, dz_j.
\end{equation}
Since the Gaussian distribution has vanaishing moments for moment larger than third order, the expression is simplified into
\begin{equation}
    \langle \hat{C}_{ij} \rangle = \hat{C}_{ij} + \frac{1}{2} \, \, \sigma_{i}^2 \, \, \partial^2_{ii} \, C_{ij}|_{(\hat{z}_i, \hat{z}_j)} \, \, + \frac{1}{2} \, \, \sigma_{j}^2 \, \, \partial^2_{jj} \, C_{ij}|_{(\hat{z}_i, \hat{z}_j)}.
\end{equation}
Therefore, it is possible to account for the error in the covariance due to redshift uncertainty using the second derivative of the covariance with respect to redshift $z$. In chapter \ref{chap:results}, I will show the impact to redshift uncertainty relative to the case with correction covariance.

\subsection{Unlocalised hosts}
I simulate unlocalised FRBs in a fashion similar to what is descibed in seciotn \ref{theory_sec:uncertainties}. To fill in missing redshift, I use the fiducial parameters in order to obtain an expectation of the unknown redshift $z_i = z(\mathrm{DM}_i)$ from the measured $\mathrm{DM}_i$. To propagate the error to the redshift space, I assume that $\mathrm{DM}$ follows a lognormal distribution with underlying Gaussian mean  $\mathrm{DM}_i$ and variance $\sigma^2(z_i)$, such that
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{DM}_i \sim P(\mathrm{DM}; \mu^i, \sigma_{\mathrm{LN}, i}^2)
\end{equation}
with 
\begin{equation}
    \mu^i = \ln\left(\frac{\mathrm{DM}_{i}^{2}}{\sqrt{\sigma^2(z^i) + \mathrm{DM}_{i}^{2}}}\right) \, , \, \sigma_{\mathrm{LN}, i}^2 = \ln\left(1 + \frac{\sigma^2(z^i)}{\mathrm{DM}_{i}^{2}}\right)
\end{equation}
The lognormal assumption is valid in the case of this work since the $\mathrm{DM}$ contributions from the different shells are lognormal. After that, I sample $\mathrm{sample}$ from the distribution, and I use the same mean DM-$z$ relation to map the sampled DM to $z$-space. After that, I calculate the sample variance of the mapped redshifts $\sigma^2_{i}$, where $\sigma_i/\sqrt{n_{\mathrm{sample}}}$ is the error on the estimated redshift $z_i$.

To propagate the error to the covarirance, I use the same formalism introduced in section \ref{meth_sec:z_uncertainty}, only as an approximation, since the underlying PDF of the estimated redshift is not a Gaussian distribution. For the values of $\sigma_{ii}$, if the FRB has an undefined host, the redshift uncertainty is calculated with the method introduced before. Meanwhile, if the redshift is exact, then I set the value of $\sigma_{ii} = 0$. In this theis, I aim to disentangle the different systematic uncertainties. To qunatity the impact of systematic uncertainty on the DM-$z$ relation, I compare a fiducial catalog with a contaminated catalog using the $\chi^2$ test. In particular, the maximum $\chi^2$ estimator is a method to infer model parameters from data by comparing the difference between data $d$ the mean predicted by the model parameters $\mu(\theta)$ given some weight for every point. In a flat geometry, the $\chi^2$ can be written as 
\begin{equation}
    \chi^2 = \sum_{i} w_i(d_i - \mu_i(\theta))^2.
\end{equation}
If data points are correalted, the $\chi^2$ can be written in terms of the weights $w_{ij}$ such that
\begin{equation}
    \chi^2 = \sum_{ij} (d_i - \mu_i(\theta))w_{ij}(d_j - \mu_j(\theta)).
\end{equation}
It can be shown that the minimum variane $\chi^2$ estimator is the estimator with $w_{ij}$ being the inverse of the data covariance matrix $C_{ij}$, making the final expression of the $\chi^2$
\begin{equation}
    \chi^2 = \sum_{ij} (d_i - \mu_i(\theta))C_{ij}^{-1}(d_j - \mu_j(\theta)).
\end{equation}
The probability that a value $\chi^2$ will exceed the true $\hat{\chi}^2$ is given by 

\begin{equation}
    Q(\nu, \hat{\chi}) = 1 - \Gamma(\nu/2, \hat{\chi}/2), 
\end{equation}
where $\Gamma$ is the incomplete $\Gamma$-function and $\nu$ is the number of degrees of freedom. In this thesis, the number of degrees of freedom equals to the number of FRBs in a catalog.

\section{Simulation-based inference} \label{meth_sec:sbi}
To forecast the bias in parameter estimation, I use simulation based inference (SBI) method. A review of simulation baed inference can be found in \citet{cranmer2020frontier} and SBI lecture by Noemi Anau Montel \footnote{\href{https://wwwmpa.mpa-garching.mpg.de/~komatsu/lecturenotes/Noemi_Anau_Montel_on_SBI.pdf}{The Postdoc lecture by Noemi Anau Montel}} given at the Postdoc Lecture Series at the Max Planck Institute for Astrophysics. SBI is a method to infer the parameter of a model by compared an observed data vector $d$ with  a data vector obtained from a forward simulation. It is an extension of Baysian inference in which the parameter posterior is constrained by performing Monte Carlo Markov Chain (MCMC) to obtain the parameter's posterior distribution. If the prior of the model parameter is $P(\theta)$ and the likelihood of data given the parameter is $P(d|\theta)$ then the posterior distribution of the parameters given data $P(\theta|d)$ is written by Bayes rule
\begin{equation}
    P(\theta|d) \sim P(d|\theta) \, P(\theta).
\end{equation}
In traditional cosmological application, there are two consideration. First, by invoking the central limit theorem (CLT)\footnote{Central limit theorem (CLT) states that the probability distribution function of indepedent and identically distributed random variables approaches a Gaussian distribution as the number of sampled variables approaches infinity if the true probability distribution funciton has a finite variance.}, $P(d|\theta)$ can be assumed to be a multivariate Gaussian. However, this is not necessairly true if the conditions of CLT are not fullfilled, leaving the problem with intractable likelihood. 

To solve this problem, Approximate Baysian Computation (ABC) can be used to obtain posterior sample without a tractable likelihood and using a forward model. By defining a distance caliber between an the a data vector $\mathbf{x_0}$ and a data vector form the forward model $\mathbf{x}$ such that
\begin{equation}
    d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x_0}) = ||\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x_0}||.
\end{equation}
The samples from the approximate posterior are drawn with rejection sampling using an acceptance tolerance rate $\epsilon$ such that accepted samples satify $d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x_0}) < \epsilon$, and the posterior is given By
\begin{equation}
    p_{\mathrm{ABC}}(\Theta \mid \boldsymbol{x}) = \frac{\int_{d(\boldsymbol{x}_0, \boldsymbol{x}) < \epsilon} \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{x} \, p(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \Theta) p(\Theta)}{\int_{d(\boldsymbol{x}_0, \boldsymbol{x}) < \epsilon} \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{x} \, p(\boldsymbol{x})}
\end{equation}
is guarenteed to converge to the true posterior distributiuon. However, the sampling efficiency of this method is very low, especially if the problem involves wide priors and large parameter space. Therefore, machine learning (ML) methods are used to learn the posterior distribution of the observed data. Nonetheless, with high dimensional data such as that of FRBs, both ML and ABC methods fail to converge quickly. Therefore, the need for a summary statics that retains the cosmological information is necessairy. I describe below how to use the simulation pipeline desceibed in section \ref{meth_sec:sim_dm} to perform simulation based inference of the cosmological and the baryonic feedback paraemters.
