\chapter{Methodology} \label{chap:method}
In this section, I explain the methoddology used in this thesis. The end purpose is to show how the $DM$-$z$ relation deviates from its what is expected by contaminating mock FRBs catalog with systematic uncertainties through simulating FRBs catalogs and studying the impact of systematic uncertainties when contaminating catalogs with these systematic uncertainties. Fig. \ref{meth_fig:simulation_flow} shows a flowchart of how to generate a mock catalog. I explain the steps in details below. I perform the analysis assuming the detection for $N_\mathrm{FRBs} = 10^4$. This is the expected number of detected FRBs per year by future FRBs survey such as ther SKAO \citep{ macquart2015fast}. Future work should focus on scaling up to larger sample size since the impact of systematic uncertainties changes with sample size.

To model the dispersion measure signal, I use the Generator for Large-Scale Structure (GLASS) \citep{tessore2023glass} package. I describe this package in further details in section \ref{meth_sec:glass}. Briefly, the GLASS package generates lognormal maps of a cosmological field starting from a given angular power spectra. On contrary to the commonly used \texttt{healpy} package, it assumes that the cosmological field follows a lognormal distribution. Though this is not the case in for a real cosmological field, the GLASS package preserves the two-point statistics of the cosmologocal field, which is enough for the sample size I am considering in this thesis. Higher order statistics such as the bispectrum (3-point) and the trispectrum (4-point) requires a larger sample size for detection. Using the electron power spectrum, I project the spectra on the unit sphere to obtain an expectation of the angular power specta in bins of redshifts. After that, I feed the resultatnt power spectra to GLASS in order to obtain maps of electron density in bins of redshift. THe next step constitute in sampling randomly on the sky and according to the source redshift distribution in redshift then sum the contribution from different shells. \subsection{Redshift uncertainty} \label{meth_sec:z_uncertainty}
I simulate the imapct of redshift uncertainty by assuming that a redshift measuremnet $z_i$ is sampled from a Gaussian distribution with mean $z_i$ and standard deviation $\sigma_i = \eta z_i$, where $\eta_i$ is a number between $0$ and $1$. To propagate the error on the cosmological covariance, consider that $(z_i, z_j)  \sim \mathcal{N}((\hat{z}_i, \hat{z}_j) , \Sigma)$, with $\mathcal{N}$ is the normal distribution and
\begin{equation}
    \Sigma = \begin{bmatrix}
    \sigma_i^2 & 0 \\
    0 & \sigma_j^2 
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
To propagate the error, I calcualte the expectation value $\langle \hat{C}_{ij} \rangle$, given as
\begin{equation}
    \langle \hat{C}_{ij} \rangle = \int \mathcal{N}((\hat{z}_i, \hat{z}_j) , \Sigma) \, \, C_{ij} \, \,dz_i \, \, dz_j
\end{equation}
With Taylor's expansion around $(\hat{z}_i, \hat{z}_j)$ and setting $\Delta_{ij} = (z_i, z_j) - (\hat{z}_i, \hat{z}_j)$, the covariance matrix can be written
\begin{equation}
    \langle \hat{C}_{ij} \rangle = \int \mathcal{N} \, \, \left(\hat{C}_{ij} + \nabla C_{ij}|_{(\hat{z}_i, \hat{z}_j)} \Delta_{ij} + \frac{1}{2} \Delta_{ij}^{T} \nabla^2 C_{ij}|_{(\hat{z}_i, \hat{z}_j)}\Delta_{ij} + .....\right)\, \,dz_i \, \, dz_j.
\end{equation}
Since the Gaussian distribution has vanaishing moments for moment larger than third order, the expression is simplified into
\begin{equation}
    \langle \hat{C}_{ij} \rangle = \hat{C}_{ij} + \frac{1}{2} \, \, \sigma_{i}^2 \, \, \partial^2_{ii} \, C_{ij}|_{(\hat{z}_i, \hat{z}_j)} \, \, + \frac{1}{2} \, \, \sigma_{j}^2 \, \, \partial^2_{jj} \, C_{ij}|_{(\hat{z}_i, \hat{z}_j)}.
\end{equation}
Therefore, it is possible to account for the error in the covariance due to redshift uncertainty using the second derivative of the covariance with respect to redshift $z$.

I show the impact of redshift uncertainty on the scatter in the DM-$z$ relation in chapter \ref{chap:results}.

\subsection{Unlocalised hosts}
I simulate unlocalised FRBs in a fashion similar to what is descibed in seciotn \ref{theory_sec:uncertainties}. To fill in missing redshift, I use the fiducial parameters in order to obtain an expectation of the unknown redshift $z_i = z(\mathrm{DM}_i)$ from the measured $\mathrm{DM}_i$. To propagate the error to the redshift space, I assume that $\mathrm{DM}$ follows a lognormal distribution with underlying Gaussian mean  $\mathrm{DM}_i$ and variance $\sigma^2(z_i)$, such that
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{DM}_i \sim P(\mathrm{DM}; \mu^i, \sigma_{\mathrm{LN}, i}^2)
\end{equation}
with 
\begin{equation}
    \mu^i = \ln\left(\frac{\mathrm{DM}_{i}^{2}}{\sqrt{\sigma^2(z^i) + \mathrm{DM}_{i}^{2}}}\right) \, , \, \sigma_{\mathrm{LN}, i}^2 = \ln\left(1 + \frac{\sigma^2(z^i)}{\mathrm{DM}_{i}^{2}}\right)
\end{equation}
The lognormal assumption is valid in the case of this work since the $\mathrm{DM}$ contributions from the different shells are lognormal. After that, I sample $\mathrm{sample}$ from the distribution, and I use the same mean DM-$z$ relation to map the sampled DM to $z$-space. After that, I calculate the sample variance of the mapped redshifts $\sigma^2_{i}$, where $\sigma_i/\sqrt{n_{\mathrm{sample}}}$ is the error on the estimated redshift $z_i$.

To propagate the error to the covarirance, I use the same formalism introduced in section \ref{meth_sec:z_uncertainty}, only as an approximation, since the underlying PDF of the estimated redshift is not a Gaussian distribution. For the values of $\sigma_{ii}$, if the FRB has an undefined host, the redshift uncertainty is calculated with the method introduced before. Meanwhile, if the redshift is exact, then I set the value of $\sigma_{ii} = 0$. In this theis, I aim to disentangle the different systematic uncertainties. In reality, These effects could be degenerate in the way they impact the measured DM or the covariance. I discuss that futher in chapter \ref{chap:discuss}.
